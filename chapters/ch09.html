GOLDEN.

The mashed potatoes were cold.

Gianna noticed because she'd been pushing them around her plate for ten minutes while monitoring the temperature of the room. Laura sat at the head of the table, hands wrapped around a wine glass she'd refilled twice. Nico sat across from Gianna, fork in hand, not eating. Just staring at his plate.

The silence had stretched for 47 seconds.

"So," Gianna said brightly, "I made the dean's List last semester."

Laura's head snapped up, grateful for the lifeline. "Did you? That's wonderful, sweetheart." Her smile was genuine for half a second. "I'm so proud of you."

"Thanks. It wasn't easy—Econ almost killed me—but I pulled it off."

"You always do." Laura's gaze drifted toward Nico. "You've always been so good at school."

Gianna felt the shift immediately. The way Laura's compliment was edging toward comparison. She redirected fast.

"How's work, Mom? You mentioned they were doing layoffs?"

Laura blinked. Refocused. "Oh. Yes. They let go of three people in my department last week. It's been tense."

"That's awful. Are you worried about your position?"

"A little. But I've been there ten years, so..." She trailed off. Gianna kept her talking. Asked about her coworkers. About her boss. Small details that kept Laura's attention away from the fact that Nico hadn't said a word in five minutes.

But eventually, Laura's gaze drifted back.

"Nico," she said carefully, "how's school?"

"Fine."

One word. Clipped. Laura's jaw tightened.

"Just fine?" Laura pressed.

"His architecture professor is really impressed with his work, actually. Mr. Trager, right?" She looked at Nico. "You mentioned he praised your last project?"

Nico glanced at her. Recognized what she was doing. "Yeah."

"See?" Gianna smiled at Laura. "He's doing well."

Laura softened slightly. "That's good. I'm glad. I just worry. You don't answer my calls, you don't respond to texts—"

"Because you always ask the same questions," Nico said.

"Because you won't talk to me!"

"Mom," Gianna said gently, "I know you're worried. But interrogating him isn't going to help."

Laura's eyes softened. "I'm not interrogating. I'm asking."

"I know. But maybe..." Gianna chose her words carefully. "Maybe give him space to share when he's ready?"

Laura pressed her lips together. Looked at Nico. "Are you ever going to be ready?"

The question hung there. Raw. Painful.

Nico stared at his plate for a long moment. Then: "I started therapy."

Laura stared at him. "What?"

"Therapy. I found someone on campus. Dr. Reeves. I've been going for a few weeks."

Gianna's fork clattered against her plate. "You didn't tell me that."

Nico glanced at her, then back at Laura. "I'm telling you now."

Laura's hand went to her mouth. Her eyes shone. "You're actually going?"

"Yeah."

"On your own? You set it up yourself?"

"Yeah."

"I..." Laura's voice broke. "That's really good, Nico. That's really good."

Gianna felt relief wash over her, he was getting help. He'd done it himself. This was good.

But underneath: Why didn't he tell me?

"How's it going?" Laura asked, voice soft now.

"It's fine."

"Are you going to keep going?"

"I have another appointment tomorrow."

Laura nodded, blinking rapidly. "Thank you for telling me."

He shrugged. "It's just therapy."

"It's not just therapy," Laura said. "It's taking care of yourself. That matters."

The tension in the room shifted. Still heavy, but less volatile.

Gianna kept the conversation moving. Asked Nico about his other classes. Asked Laura about her sister in Seattle. Filled the gaps. Smoothed the rough edges. Kept everyone talking so no one had time to retreat into silence or explode into arguments.

By the time Laura stood to get dessert, Gianna's jaw ached from smiling. Her shoulders were tight. She felt like she'd been holding up a ceiling.

In the kitchen, she heard dishes clinking. Heard Laura take a shaky breath.

Nico looked at Gianna. "Thanks."

"For what?"

"You know what."

She did. But it exhausted her to hear it said out loud.

Laura returned with store-bought pie. They ate in careful conversation—Gianna steering, redirecting, managing. By the time the pie was gone, it was almost 8:00.

The dining room was still too quiet. The kind of quiet that amplified everything. Gianna looked at the empty chair at the other end of the table.

Dad would have filled this space, made them laugh. Would have asked real questions and gotten real answers. But he wasn't there.

He'd never been there. Not in this house.

By the time they left, it was almost 9:00.

Nico grabbed his jacket from the couch. Laura stood in the doorway, arms crossed like she was holding herself together.

"You'll call me this week?" she asked Gianna.

"Yeah."

"And you'll make sure he—"

"I'll check in. I promise."

Laura nodded. Looked at Nico. "Be safe driving home."

"We're taking Gianna's car," Nico said.

"Oh. Right." She hesitated. "Well. Be safe."

"Okay."

They left. The door closed behind them with a soft click that felt louder than it should.

Outside, the air was cold and damp. Typical Olympia. Gianna unlocked her car. They got in without speaking.

She started the engine. Pulled out of the driveway. Drove in silence for three blocks before Nico finally said, "Thanks."

Gianna kept her eyes on the road. "She's just worried."

"I know."

"She doesn't know how to talk to you."

"I know."

More silence. The wipers moved across the windshield in a steady rhythm. Squeak. Squeak. Squeak.

"Do you think it'll ever get easier?" Nico asked.

"I don't know."

"That's not reassuring."

"You didn't ask me to be reassuring. You asked if I thought it'd get easier."

Nico almost smiled. Almost.

They pulled up outside his apartment building. Gianna put the car in park but didn't turn off the engine.

"You want to come up for a bit?" Nico asked.

Gianna glanced at the clock. 9:17. She had homework. Had to be up early tomorrow.

"Yeah. For a bit."

Nico's apartment looked the same as it always did. No dishes in the sink. No clutter. Just empty space and the faint smell of the lemon cleaner he used on everything.

Loaf appeared immediately, winding between Nico's legs, meowing loudly.

"I know, I know," Nico muttered, heading to the kitchen to feed her.

Gianna sat on the couch. Sank into the cushions. Felt the exhaustion hit all at once.

Nico came back, sat in the armchair across from her.

"How are you really doing?" Gianna asked.

"I'm fine. Therapy's helping. Sort of."

"Sort of?"

He shrugged. "Dr. Reeves keeps asking me to talk about feelings. I'm not great at that."

"Shocking."

"But I'm trying." He looked at her. "I am."

They sat in silence for a minute. Loaf's purring filled the space.

"I miss him," Gianna whispered.

"Me too."

"I keep thinking about dumb stuff," Gianna said. "Like how he'd make pancakes on Sunday mornings. And they'd always be uneven because he'd flip them too early."

Nico's lips twitched. Almost a smile. "He couldn't cook to save his life."

"No. But he tried."

"Yeah." More silence. But this time it was softer.

" She sat up. "I should go. I have class early tomorrow." Gianna stood and grabbed her bag.

Nico stood too. Loaf protested the movement with an indignant meow.

At the door, Gianna paused. "You'll actually go to therapy tomorrow?"

"Yeah."

"Promise?"

"Promise."

She nodded. "Text me after?"

"Okay." They hugged, and she left.

The drive back to campus was quiet.

Gianna rolled the windows down despite the cold. Her phone buzzed at a red light. Mom.

Thank you for coming tonight. It meant a lot. Please check on your brother this week.

She didn't respond. Just set the phone in the cup holder and kept driving.

The campus was mostly dark when she arrived. A few students walking back from the library. She parked, grabbed her bag, and headed inside.

Her dorm room was empty. Jess was probably at her boyfriend's. Gianna dropped her bag, kicked off her shoes, and just stood there in the middle of the room. Dinner had been two hours. Just two hours. But she felt like she'd run a marathon.

She pulled out her phone. Opened her calendar. Looked at the week ahead.

Monday: Classes, study group, check in with Nico.
Tuesday: Econ, coffee with debate team, call Mom.
Wednesday: Classes, group project meeting, check in with Nico.
Thursday: Psychology, study session, call Mom.
Friday: Classes, debate practice, check in with Nico.

Every day. Something. Someone. She closed the calendar. Opened her messages. Sofia had texted her that afternoon: Hey! How's your weekend going?

Gianna had read it. Hadn't responded.

She typed: It was okay. Yours?

Three dots appeared immediately.

Pretty good! Went hiking with some friends. Got caught in the rain lol

That sounds fun

Yeah! Hey, want to grab coffee this week? I know you're busy, but maybe we could study together or something?

Gianna stared at the message. Wanted to say yes. Wanted it so badly it hurt.

Typed: I'm pretty swamped with the midterm, but maybe after?

The dots appeared. Disappeared. Appeared again.

Sure! Just let me know :)

Gianna set the phone down. She changed into pajamas. Brushed her teeth. Got into bed. Stared at the ceiling.

Her phone buzzed one more time. Nico.

Thanks for tonight. Love you.

She typed back: Love you too.

Set the phone on her nightstand. Closed her eyes.

Gianna lay there in the dark and whispered to no one: "I'm tired."

But tomorrow, she'd get up. She'd go to class. She'd check in on Nico. She'd call her mom.

She'd be golden.

Because that's what she did.
