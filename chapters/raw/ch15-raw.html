Blackberries.

They’re just berries,

he tells himself, stains covering his fingers.

The juice seeps into the creases of his skin, dark and stubborn. No matter how many times he wipes his hands against his jeans, faint smudges linger—Pink-Purple ghosts press into the fabric. He pops another berry into his mouth. Tartness blooms across his tongue, the kind that tightens the jaw before sweetness follows, slow and inevitable.

He sighs. He didn't eat blackberries before. They're a remnant of someone he used to know. A snack you never used to eat. A song you can't listen to anymore. A route home taken out of habit rather than choice.

The taste sours as the thought creeps in, turning something simple into something heavier. He considers throwing the rest away. He could just drop the carton in the nearest trash can and walk off. But he doesn’t. Of course, he doesn’t. They’re just berries. His gaze drops to his jeans, the faint smears dull against the fabric. He wonders how many washes, how many months or years it’ll take for this stain to give way.

He glances back at the berries. The carton sits open on the bench beside him, juice pooling at the bottom.

They’re just berries.

Someone introduced him to blackberries. At one point this was new to him. “You’ve never had them?” she laughed, disbelief bright in her voice. “You don’t know what you’re missing.” She popped one into his mouth from the passenger seat before he could protest. Tartness first. Sweetness after. “Life’s like that”, she said.” Sometimes you have to get through the first punch to get to the good part.”

He hadn’t believed her then. Not fully. But he wanted to..

He wipes his stained fingers against his jeans again. The smudges have darkened, seeping in deeper. Months of washing wouldn’t erase them. Maybe nothing would.

They’re just berries.

There's a space that opens up after someone leaves. Not a clean, sharp hole—but something messier. A gap you don't notice until you catch yourself reaching for something that isn't there anymore. He sighed, fingers running through his hair, gaze drifting to the carton beside him. Blackberries.

He picked one up. Stained fingers. Stained jeans. At what point was he stained, at what point were the stains him?

Tartness hit his tongue, sharp and immediate. Sweetness followed. Slow. Inevitable. Life’s like that, she’d said. And maybe she’d been right. The bitterness wasn’t something you avoided; you tasted it, let it settle, and trusted the sweetness to follow. Trusted that it was worth it.

He chewed slowly, the juice pooling along the edges of his mouth. Another berry. Stains deepening. They wouldn’t come out—not entirely. They weren’t supposed to. He closed the carton. Sat there for a long moment, the world moving on around him, indifferent to the thoughts he had. It felt like even the wind thought:

They’re just berries.
