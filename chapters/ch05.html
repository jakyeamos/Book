Friday, March.

THE GIRL WITH THE HAMMER.

Nico

The first time Emma asked him to get coffee, he said no. It was a Friday in March. Class had just ended. Students were packing up, making weekend plans, their voices bright with the promise of two days without homework.

Emma stopped at Nico's desk. "Hey. Want to get coffee? Compare notes for the midterm?"

His immediate instinct: no. "I have to get home," he said.

"Okay." She didn't look hurt. Just nodded. "Maybe another time." She walked away, and Nico gathered his things slowly.

11 PM.

Later that night, he found himself downstairs at 11 PM, unable to sleep. The house was dark except for the dining room, where light spilled from under the door. He pushed it open. Luca sat at the table, surrounded by papers and photographs. His laptop was open, three different documents visible. Coffee cup half-empty despite the late hour.

"Can't sleep either?" Luca asked without looking up.

"Thirsty." Nico went to the kitchen, got water, but instead of going back upstairs, he found himself sitting across from his father. Luca glanced at him. "You okay?"

"Yeah." They sat in silence for a minute. Luca typed something, then deleted it. Typed again.

"What are you working on?" Nico asked.

"That wetlands development story. I'm close. Really close. Just need to confirm a few more details."

"Is it important?"

Luca looked up, meeting his eyes. "I think so. Someone's cutting corners, falsifying reports. If they keep building there, the whole area could be contaminated. People deserve to know."

Nico nodded, not really understanding.

"How's school?" Luca asked.

"Fine."

"Just fine?"

Pause. Nico stood. "I'm tired. Going to bed."

"Nico—"

"Night, Dad."

He left Luca sitting there, returned to his room.

Next Monday.

Next Monday, Emma asked again. "Coffee? Just to study. I promise I won't be weird."

Nico looked at her. She was smiling, but there was something tentative underneath. Like she was preparing for another no.

"Yeah," he said. "Okay."Emma's face lit up.

"Really? Saturday?"

“Sounds good.” He surprised himself with every word that escaped his mouth

"Saturday. Great." She was already walking backward toward the door, grinning. "I'll text you."

Nico was confused, “I don’t think you have my number.”

“I do, Gianna gave it to me,” she said as she left, and Nico sat there not knowing how to feel.

Saturday.

Saturday arrived too quickly. Nico changed his shirt three times. Told himself it didn't matter. Changed it again. Left fifteen minutes early because being late made his chest tight.

"Hey!" She was wearing an oversized sweater and ripped jeans. Her hair was up in a messy bun.

They ordered. Emma got something with caramel and whipped cream that barely qualified as coffee. Nico got a green tea. They found a table in the back corner. Spread out textbooks and notebooks. Stared at them for thirty seconds.

"I don't want to study."

Nico chuckled. "Then why did you ask?"

"Because I wanted to hang out with you, but 'want to hang out' felt too direct and scary." She shrugged. "Coffee and studying felt safer."

"For who?"

"For both of us, I guess."

She wasn't wrong. Emma pushed her textbook aside. "Want to see something?"

"Okay." She pulled out a sketchbook—not the one she used for class, a different one. Personal. She flipped it open to a random page. It was a drawing of the coffee shop they were sitting in. But not realistic—abstracted, dreamlike. The people were just shapes and colors. The light was exaggerated, pouring through windows that didn't exist in the real space. It felt warm. She turned pages. More drawings—a park, a street corner, someone's living room. Each one captured something intangible. Mood. Atmosphere. Emotion.

"These are really good," Nico said.

"Thanks. Your turn."

"My turn?"

"Show me something you don't show people."

They talked for two hours. When Emma checked her phone, it was 4:15.

"Oh shit. I told my mom I'd be home at four."

"Sorry, I didn't mean to—"

"Don't apologize. This was nice." She stood, gathered her stuff. "We should do it again."

"Yeah."

Emma smiled—wide and genuine. "Good. Text me?"

"Okay."

Nico learned things about her:

She missed Oakland, but not enough to go back.

She hummed to herself while drawing.

She laughed at her own jokes before the punchline.

Emma learned things about him:

He didn't sleep well.

He liked rain because it gave him a reason to stay inside.

He didn't trust good things to last.

They didn't talk about that last part explicitly. But Emma noticed. The way he'd pull back whenever they got too comfortable. The way he'd find excuses to end conversations early.

May.

May arrived, and with it, something shifted.

They were at Emma's house on a Saturday afternoon, theoretically studying for finals. Emma's parents were out. Her room was exactly what Nico expected—art everywhere, clothes on the floor, organized chaos that somehow made sense.

They'd been working in silence for twenty minutes when Emma threw her pencil down.

"I can't focus."

"Me neither."

"Want to take a break?"

"Yeah."

They migrated to her bed. Emma pulled up a playlist on her phone. Something indie and mellow. They lay on their backs, staring at her ceiling, not talking.

Nico's hand rested near hers. Not touching. But close.

Emma spoke first. "Can I ask you something?"

"Okay."

"Why do you pull away?"

"What?"

"Sometimes we're good. And then suddenly you're distant. Like you're reminding yourself not to get too close." She turned her head to look at him. "Why?"

Nico kept his eyes on the ceiling. "I don't know."

"Is it me?"

"No. It's not you."

"Then what?"

How could he explain? The certainty that he ruined everything he touched? That his parents fight because of him? That anyone who got close would eventually realize he wasn't worth the effort?

"I'm not good at this," he said instead.

"At what?"

"People. Relationships. Letting anyone in."

"You're doing okay with me."

"Am I?"

"Yeah." She reached over, found his hand. Laced her fingers through his. "You're here, aren't you?"

Emma squeezed his hand.

"Or maybe we could try trusting this. Just for a while."

"What if I mess it up?"

"What if you don't?"

They lay there, hands linked, music playing softly. The afternoon light coming through her window painted everything golden.

"Emma?"

"Yeah?"

"Can I kiss you?"

She smiled. "I thought you'd never ask."

He leaned over. She met him halfway. Their lips touched—tentative, soft, testing. Emma's hand moved to his face, thumb tracing his cheekbone. Nico's free hand found her waist.

The kiss deepened. Became something more certain. She tasted like the cherry Chapstick she always wore. When she made a small sound against his mouth, Nico felt it everywhere.

They broke apart. Foreheads touching. Both breathing hard.

"Okay?" Emma whispered.

"Yeah."

"I'm doing an art show," she said.

"You are?"

"Small thing. Local gallery. They have these monthly showcases for student artists. I submitted, and they accepted three of my pieces."

"Emma, that's amazing."

She shrugged, but she was smiling. "It's next Saturday. 6 PM. You should come."

"I'll be there."

"You don't have to—"

"I'll be there," he repeated.

Next Saturday. 6 PM.

The gallery was small, tucked between a vintage clothing store and a Thai restaurant. When Nico arrived, it was already crowded—art students, parents, people who looked like they knew about art and had opinions.

Nico found Emma near the back, standing with a group of people he didn't know. She saw him, excused herself, and came over.

"You came."

"I said I would."

"I know, but..." She glanced back at her friends. "Want me to introduce you?"

His immediate instinct: no. Too many new people. Too much small talk. Too much pretending to be normal.

But Emma was looking at him with something like hope.

"Okay," he said.

She took his hand—casual, comfortable—and led him over.

"Everyone, this is Nico. Nico, this is Maya, Jordan, and Alex. We all take art together."

"Hey," Nico said.

They said hey back. Asked how he knew Emma. Asked if he did art too. He managed answers that didn't sound completely awkward. They were nice. Easy. Didn't pry.

After a few minutes, they drifted back into their conversation about some artist Nico had never heard of. Emma leaned close.

"You okay?"

"Yeah."

"You sure? We can leave if—"

"I'm okay. Show me your pieces."

Emma's work was displayed on the far wall. Three drawings, matted and framed. The coffee shop. A street corner at sunset. Someone's living room, empty but full of implied presence.

Nico stood in front of them for a long time.

"What do you think?" Emma asked quietly.

"I think you see things no one else sees."

"Is that good?"

"Yeah. It's really good."

July.

One night in July, they were watching something forgettable. Nico's head was on Emma's shoulder, her fingers absently playing with his hair. Gianna had fallen asleep in the armchair.

"Your sister snores," Emma whispered.

"Tell her that. I dare you."

Emma laughed softly.

"Can I ask you something?" she said.

"Okay."

"Is this... are we...?" She paused. "I don't know how to ask this."

"Are we together? Like, officially?"

Late July.

In late July, Nico brought Emma home for dinner. He'd been putting it off. The idea of his family and Emma in the same room felt dangerous.

But Emma asked, and Gianna backed her up, and eventually he ran out of excuses.

"It'll be fine," Gianna assured him. "'Colo, I promise. Just a normal dinner."

Nothing in the Andretti house was ever just normal. But that night, somehow, it was close.

Luca was home on time. Laura had cooked pasta with marinara, garlic bread, and salad. The table was actually set. Gianna had even lit candles, which felt excessive, but Emma seemed charmed.

"So, Emma," Luca said as they ate. "Gianna tells me you're an artist."

"Trying to be," Emma said. "I had a show last month. Small thing, but it was cool."

"That's wonderful. What kind of art?"

"Drawings, mostly. I like capturing spaces. How places feel rather than what they look like."

"Interesting. There's something very architectural about that. Right, Nico?"

Nico nodded, grateful his dad was carrying the conversation.

After dinner, when Nico walked her to her car later, Emma was smiling.

"Your family's interesting," she said.

"That's one word for it."

"I like them. Your dad's sweet. Your mom tries really hard. Gianna's hilarious."

"They were on their best behavior."

"Still." She leaned against her car. "I'm glad I got to see where you come from."

"Even though it's kind of a mess?"

"Everyone's family is kind of a mess." She reached for his hand. "Thanks for letting me in."

He kissed her. Right there in his driveway, under the porch light, not caring if anyone saw.

"Thank you for staying," he said.

August.

August came with the slow realization that summer was ending. Senior year loomed. College applications would be due soon. Everything felt temporary, like they were all holding their breath before the next thing.

One evening, the whole family was home for dinner—a rare occurrence that felt almost orchestrated. Luca had been working late for weeks, but tonight he'd come home at 5:30, taken off his shoes, and announced he was cooking.

"What's the occasion?" Laura asked, suspicious.

"Can't I just cook for my family?"

"You can. You usually don't."

Over dinner, the conversation was lighter than usual. Gianna talked about her upcoming junior year. Nico mentioned his architecture project had been selected for a student showcase.

Then Luca cleared his throat.

"So I wanted to tell you all something."

"I'm almost done with the wetlands story. The big one I've been working on for months."

"It is." He looked around the table, landed on Laura. "And after it's published, I'm taking a break."

"A real one. At least a month, maybe more. No big stories. No late nights. No phone calls at 2 AM from sources. I haven't been around enough. I know that. I'm going to fix it."

"I've heard that before," Laura said quietly.

"I know. But I mean it this time. This story—it's important. It matters. But so do you. So do the kids. I've been letting work consume everything, and that's not okay."

Laura didn't say anything for a long moment. Then: "Okay."

It wasn't forgiveness. Not quite. But it was something. A tentative hope that maybe things could be different.

Gianna caught Nico's eye across the table. He could see her thinking the same thing he was: Maybe this time it'll stick. Maybe things are getting better.

After dinner, when everyone had scattered to their rooms, Nico lay on his bed and thought about senior year starting in two weeks. Emma would be there. Gianna would be a year behind him, still around. His parents might actually figure their shit out. College applications were scary but also exciting—a way forward, a way out.

For the first time in years, the future didn't feel like something to dread.

It felt like a possibility.
