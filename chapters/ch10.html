GIANNA'S WEEK.

Olympia, Washington
 Monday, March 9 - Thursday, March 12, 2036

Monday

Gianna's alarm went off at 6:45 AM. She hit snooze twice before dragging herself out of bed.

Jess was back and already awake, sitting at her desk with headphones on, painting her nails a shade of purple that looked like it belonged in a galaxy. She looked up when Gianna stirred.

"Hey! You visited your brother last night?"

"Yeah."

"How is he?"

"He's okay." Gianna grabbed her towel. "Going to therapy."

"Oh, that's good!" Jess went back to her nails. "My ex went to therapy. Didn't help, but you know. At least he tried."

Gianna didn't have the energy to unpack that. Just headed to the communal bathroom down the hall. The shower was lukewarm at best. Someone had used all the hot water. Gianna stood under the spray anyway, letting it wake her up slowly.

By 7:30, she was dressed and caffeinated. Her calendar stared at her from her phone screen:

Monday:

  • 9:00 AM: Political Science
  • 11:00 AM: Econ
  • 1:00 PM: Study group (library)
  • 3:00 PM: Office hours with Professor Bennett
  • 7:00 PM: Debate team meeting

She had seventeen minutes to get to campus.

Political Science was a blur. Professor Harris droned on about international relations theory while Gianna took notes on autopilot. Realism, liberalism, constructivism—words that meant something in theory but felt completely disconnected from the weight of Sunday dinner, from Nico's revelation about therapy, from the exhaustion sitting in her bones.

Economics was in the large lecture hall on the east side of campus. Gianna arrived early, claimed her usual seat. Other students filtered in gradually. She recognized most by now.

Sofia came in at 10:58, backpack slung over one shoulder, travel mug in hand. She wore a faded Radiohead shirt and jeans with a hole in the knee. Her hair was pulled back in a messy bun, a few curls escaping around her face.

She caught Gianna's eye. Smiled. Waved.

Gianna's stomach did that flip thing. She waved back. Sofia moved up the aisle, paused at Gianna's row. "This seat taken?"

"No. Go ahead."

Sofia sat down. "How was your weekend?"

"We had a family dinner, it was exhausting."

"That's the only kind of family dinner." Sofia laughed. It was warm. Genuine. "Yeah, I get that. My family's the same."

Gianna somehow doubted that.

Professor Wu started class before she could respond. She pulled out her notebook.

Today's topic: Labor Markets and Wage Determination.

Professor Wu pulled up a slide showing wage gaps by gender and race. "Let's talk about why women, particularly women of color, are systematically undervalued in the labor market."

Gianna took notes. Focused. Halfway through class, Sofia leaned over slightly. "Do you understand the monopsony model? I'm so lost."

Gianna glanced at her notes. "It's when there's only one buyer for labor, like a company town. They can pay workers less because there's nowhere else to go."

"Ohh. Okay. That makes sense." Sofia scribbled something in her notebook. "You're really good at this."

Gianna's face felt warm. She looked back at her notes.

After class, they packed up together. Sofia hoisted her backpack.

"You have time for coffee?" she asked. "Or are you rushing to another class?"

"I have a study group at one."

"Oh." Sofia's face fell slightly. "No worries."

They walked out together. The hallway was crowded, students flowing in every direction. The study group met in a reserved room on the third floor. Gianna arrived first, as usual. Set up her notes, pulled out extra pens, and organized the whiteboard.

The others trickled in over the next ten minutes: Tyler and Marcus (both from Poli Sci), and two girls—Aisha and Morgan—from other classes.

"You're a lifesaver," Tyler said, grabbing one of Gianna's pens. "I forgot mine. Again."

"You say that every week," Gianna pointed out.

"And yet I keep forgetting."

They settled into their usual routine. Gianna led the discussion, asked questions, kept them on track. Tyler kept getting distracted by his phone. Aisha knew the material but second-guessed herself constantly. Morgan was quiet but always had the best insights when she spoke. Marcus just wanted to get through it and leave.

"You're the best, Gianna," Aisha said as they packed up.

"Seriously," Tyler agreed. "I'd be failing without these sessions."

"You'd be fine," Gianna said.

"No, I really wouldn't."

They left. Gianna stayed behind, erasing the whiteboard, straightening chairs, leaving the room exactly as they'd found it.

Her phone buzzed. Nico.

Therapy went okay. Dr. Reeves wants me to come twice a week.

Gianna stared at the message. Waited. Wondered if he'd ask how her day was.

Nothing.

She typed back: That's good. Proud of you.

Tuesday

Debate team met Tuesday evenings at 7:00 PM. Gianna loved debate. She'd been on the team since freshman year. Started as a novice, now she was one of the varsity debaters. This semester, they were preparing for a regional tournament in April.

The team gathered in a classroom on the second floor of the humanities building. Twelve people total, split into pairs. Their coach—Professor Delgado—stood at the front, reviewing bracket predictions.

Gianna's debate partner was a junior named Marcus (different from study group Marcus). He was sharp, quick-thinking, but tended to get flustered under pressure.

"All right," Professor Delgado said. "This week's topic: Resolved, that social media platforms should be held legally liable for user-generated content. Gianna and Marcus, you're taking the affirmative."

They spent the next two hours building their case. Researching precedents. Constructing arguments. Anticipating counterpoints.

Gianna was good at this. Really good. She could see the structure of an argument like architecture—where the weight-bearing points were, where the weaknesses hid, how to build something that wouldn't collapse under scrutiny.

"That's solid," Professor Delgado said when they presented their framework. "Really solid. You two are going to do well at regionals."

Gianna felt a flicker of pride. Her phone buzzed. Sofia.

How was your day?

Gianna stared at the text. Such a simple question. But no one had asked her that. Not Mom. Not Nico.

She typed back: Long but good. I just left debate practice.

Oh cool! What do you debate about?

This week? Social media liability.

That sounds interesting. What's your position?

Affirmative. Platforms should be held liable.

I'd love to hear your arguments sometime.

Maybe after midterms?

I'm holding you to that :)

Gianna smiled. She pocketed her phone and drove back to campus.

Wednesday

Wednesday was her lightest day. Just two classes and a group project meeting for Poli Sci.

The project was about democratic backsliding in Eastern Europe. Gianna had been assigned to a group of four. She'd done most of the research. Outlined the presentation. Delegated tasks.

The meeting was in the library. Two of her group members showed up late. One didn't show at all.

"Sorry," one of them said. "I had a thing."

"It's fine." Gianna pulled out her notes. "Here's what we have so far."

She walked them through the outline. Assigned new sections. Answered questions. By the end of the hour, they had a plan.

"You're so organized," one of them said. "I wish I was like that."

"It's not that hard," Gianna said.

"It is for me."

After the meeting, Gianna had two hours before her next obligation. She went to the campus coffee shop, bought a ridiculously sugary latte, and found a table in the corner.

Opened her laptop. Stared at the blank document for her Econ paper.

Her phone buzzed. Mom.

Just checking in. How are you?

Good. Busy with school.

Have you talked to Nico this week?

He texted yesterday. He's fine.

Can you call him? Make sure he's actually going to therapy?

Gianna's jaw clenched.

He's an adult, Mom. He'll go.

I know. But can you just check? For me?

Okay.

She set the phone face down. Felt that familiar resentment rising.

A shadow fell across her table. She looked up.

Sofia stood there, tray in hand, smiling uncertainly. "Hey. Mind if I sit?"

"No. Go ahead."

Sofia sat, setting down her coffee and a muffin. "I saw you from across the room. Figured I'd say hi."

"Hi."

"Working on homework?"

"Trying to. Not making much progress."

"Yeah, I feel that." Sofia broke off a piece of muffin. "Midterms are killing me."

They sat in comfortable silence for a minute. Gianna tried to focus on her laptop, but she was hyperaware of Sofia's presence.

"Can I ask you something?" Sofia said.

"Sure."

"Do you ever just... take a break? Like, do you do anything for fun?"

Gianna blinked. "I do debate."

"Okay, but that's still academic. I mean like... fun fun. Movies. Concerts. Hiking. Anything that's just for you?"

Gianna thought about it. Couldn't come up with an answer.

"Not really," she admitted.

"That's sad."

"I'm busy."

"I know. But everyone needs downtime." Sofia leaned forward slightly. "What do you like? Like, if you had a completely free day, what would you do?"

No one had asked Gianna that in years.

"I don't know," she said honestly. "I used to like reading. Fiction. Not for class—just for fun. But I haven't done that in..."

"Since when?"

"Since my dad died."

The words came out before she could stop them.

Sofia's expression shifted. "Oh. Gianna, I'm so sorry."

"It's fine. It was a few years ago."

"That doesn't make it fine."

Gianna looked down at her laptop. "I should get back to this."

"Right. Yeah. Sorry." Sofia stood, grabbed her tray. "I'll let you work."

"Sofia—"

She turned back.

"Thank you," Gianna said. "For asking. About what I like."

Sofia smiled. "Anytime."

She left. Gianna sat there, staring at her blank document, thinking about the last time she'd read a book just because she wanted to.

She couldn't remember.

Thursday

Psychology was at 11:00 AM. Gianna arrived early, claimed her seat, pulled out her notebook.

Professor Bennett bounced into the room with his usual energy. "All right, everyone! Today we're talking about family systems and roles. This is going to get personal, so buckle up."

He pulled up a slide: Dysfunctional Family Roles

"In families dealing with trauma, addiction, or dysfunction, members often fall into specific roles. Let's start with the Hero."

Gianna's pen stopped moving.

"The Hero is the high achiever," Professor Bennett continued. "The responsible one. Looks perfect on the outside—good grades, extracurriculars, always has it together. They become the family's pride and joy. The success story."

He clicked to the next slide.

"But here's the thing: the Hero role is a performance. They're not actually okay. They're just really good at pretending. They sacrifice their own needs to make the family look functional. They become emotional caretakers. And they do it because they believe—on some level—that if they're good enough, perfect enough, responsible enough, they can fix everyone."

Gianna's hand was shaking. She set her pen down.

"But you can't fix other people," Professor Bennett said. "The Hero tries anyway. And it leads to burnout. Anxiety. Depression. Loss of identity. They don't know who they are outside of being the person who holds everything together."

A student raised her hand. "So what do they do? How do they stop?"

"Boundaries," Professor Bennett said. "The Hero has to learn they're not responsible for everyone else's well-being. That saying no doesn't make them selfish. That taking care of themselves isn't a luxury—it's necessary."

He clicked to another slide showing warning signs of Hero burnout.

Gianna read down the list:

  • Chronic exhaustion
  • Inability to say no
  • Guilt when prioritizing self
  • Resentment toward those they care for
  • Feeling responsible for others' emotions
  • Loss of personal interests/identity

Every single one.

"The Hero often doesn't realize they're in this role," Professor Bennett continued. "It feels normal to them. It's all they've known. Breaking out of it requires recognizing the pattern and actively choosing differently."

The rest of class blurred. Gianna took notes mechanically, but her mind was somewhere else.

When class ended, she packed up slowly. Students filtered out. Professor Bennett was erasing the board.

"Gianna?" He looked over. "You okay? You seemed pretty affected by today's lecture."

"I'm fine."

"You sure?"

She nodded. Forced a smile. "Just... relevant material."

"If you ever need to talk—my office hours are Tuesdays and Thursdays."

"Thanks."

She left quickly.

That evening, Gianna sat in her dorm room, staring at her calendar.

Friday:

  • Classes
  • Study session
  • Call Mom
  • Check in with Nico
  • Debate prep

Saturday:

  • Homework
  • Group project work
  • Laundry
  • Call Mom

Sunday:

  • More homework
  • Maybe see Nico?
  • Call Mom

Every day. Something. Someone. Always.

Her phone buzzed. Sofia.

Hey! Are you free this weekend? A group of us are going to this indie film screening Saturday night. Want to come?

Gianna stared at the message.

She wanted to say yes. Wanted it so badly it physically hurt.

But Saturday was homework. Group project. Obligations.

She typed: I have so much work this weekend. Maybe next time?

The dots appeared. Disappeared. Appeared again.

No worries! Let me know if you change your mind :)

Gianna set her phone down. Opened her hidden journal. Looked at her list: Things I Want

Most of it crossed out or marked "selfish."

She added a new line: To go to the movie with Sofia.

Stared at it for a long moment.

Picked up her pen.

Didn't cross it out.

Her phone buzzed again. Nico.

Had therapy again today. It's getting easier to talk.

She waited. Waited for him to ask about her.

Nothing.

She typed: That's really good. Proud of you.

Set the phone face-down.

Whispered to the empty room: "What about me?"

But she knew the answer. She was fine. She was always fine. She was golden.

That's what she did.

Outside, the campus buzzed with Thursday night energy. People heading to parties, to bars, to friends' places. Living their lives.

Gianna opened her Econ textbook and got back to work.

Tomorrow, she'd do it all again.
