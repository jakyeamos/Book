Monday, 5:30 AM.

HOPE.

Nico

The alarm went off at 5:30.

He reached over, silenced it on the first ring. Through the wall, he could already hear Gianna's shower running—the pipes groaning their usual complaint. He grabbed his phone from the nightstand.

Three texts from Emma, all sent around midnight:

Finished my college essay.
 It's probably terrible.
 TTYT <3

He smiled before he could stop himself. Typed back: It's not terrible. You're just overthinking. See you at school.

The house was cold. It always was this time of year, Sacramento's version of fall settling in with mornings that made you want to stay under the covers. But Nico swung his legs out of bed, felt his feet hit the cold floor, and stood.

Downstairs, coffee was already brewing. The smell drifted up through the vents. Nico pulled on jeans and a hoodie, ran his hand through his hair (still too long), and headed down.

The kitchen was warm. Luca sat at the table with his laptop open, glasses perched on his nose, squinting at something on the screen. He was already dressed for work—button-down, slacks, the uniform of credibility.

"Morning," he said without looking up.

"Morning."

Laura stood at the stove, flipping something in a pan. French toast, from the smell of it. She glanced over her shoulder. "Hungry?"

"Yeah."

Laura set plates in front of them. French toast, powdered sugar, sliced strawberries on the side. She squeezed Nico's shoulder as she passed. "Eat up."

They ate together. Luca asked about Gianna's debate tournament. Laura mentioned something about work. Nico contributed when prompted. He tried not to think about how rare this felt.

School.

After breakfast, Gianna drove them both to school, music blasting, her voice off-key but enthusiastic. At school, Emma was waiting by her car.

"Hey!" She was holding two coffee cups. "Got you something."

"Thanks."

They walked toward the building together. Her hand found his, and he let her take it. At the entrance, Gianna split off toward the junior hallway. "Try not to be gross," she called back.

"We'll do our best," Emma replied.

Inside, the building smelled like floor cleaner and the particular staleness of too many bodies in too small a space. Lockers slammed open and shut. Nico's locker was on the second floor. He opened it, started loading books for the first period.

Architecture.

Architecture was first period. Mr. Peterson was already at his desk when Nico arrived, reviewing notes and sipping from a travel mug that said "World's Okayest Teacher."

"Morning, Andretti. Ready to present today?"

"Present?"

"Your building model. Remember? Monday presentations for the final project."

Right. Nico had forgotten. Or maybe he'd blocked it out. Public speaking wasn't his thing.

"I guess."

"You'll do great. Your design is solid."

Other students filtered in. Emma arrived, slid into the seat next to him. "You're presenting today?"

"Apparently."

"You'll be fine. Your library is amazing."

When Nico's turn came, he stood at the front of the room with his model—a small library, lots of windows, an open floor plan, reading nooks tucked into corners. His hands shook slightly as he explained the structural elements, the design philosophy, and why he'd made certain choices.

Mr. Peterson listened intently, nodding. When Nico finished, he smiled.

"Excellent work, Andretti. The structural integrity is sound, but what impresses me most is the intentionality. You've thought about how people would use this space. That's what separates good design from great design."

Nico's face felt hot. "Thank you."

"Seriously. Keep this up."

After School.

At lunch, he pulled out his phone and took a photo of the model. Sent it to his dad. Luca responded immediately: That's incredible, 'Colo. I'm so proud of you.

After school, Emma was waiting by her car. "Want a ride home?"

"Gianna's probably waiting for me."

"She texted me. Said she's staying late for debate practice."

"She told you that?"

"Yeah. We text sometimes. Is that weird?"

"A little."

They drove to Nico's house with the windows down, music playing low. Emma's hand rested on the center console, and his found it without thinking.

"Good day?" she asked.

"Yeah. Actually, yeah."

"Your presentation went well."

"Thanks to you. You helped me with the design."

"I gave you one suggestion. You did all the work."

They pulled up to his house, and Emma turned off the engine but didn't move to get out.

"Can I ask you something?" she said. "Are you happy?"

Nico thought about it. The word happy felt too big, too loaded. But satisfied? Content? Not actively miserable?

"I think so. Yeah."

Emma smiled. "Good."

He got out, watched her drive away, then headed inside. The house was quiet. Nico grabbed a snack from the kitchen and headed upstairs.

In his room, he opened his laptop and pulled up college applications. He'd been putting them off, but deadlines were approaching. Architecture programs at various schools—some in California, some out of state. He filled in a few more fields, stared at the essay prompts.

Describe a challenge you've overcome.

He thought about the past few years. The panic attacks. The depression. The constant weight of feeling like he didn't deserve anything good. Meeting Emma. Learning to let someone in.

Maybe he could write about that.

His phone buzzed. Gianna. Mom's making dinner. Be downstairs by 7:30.

He kept working on the application, words coming easier than usual. By the time 7:00 rolled around, he'd written three paragraphs he didn't completely hate.

Downstairs, the smell of garlic and onions filled the house. His mom was at the stove, and his dad's car was in the driveway. He heard his name ring from downstairs, and he made his way to the dining room, where lively conversation and even jokes filled the room. Nico joined them at the table.
