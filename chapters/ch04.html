Nico, Age 16

A LIBRARY FOR ONE.

Nico

Nico's first class ended at 9:45. He had fifteen minutes before architecture.

Most people used that time to socialize. Nico went straight to Mr. Peterson's classroom. He set down his bag, pulled out his sketchbook, and started working. The library design was coming along. He'd spent most of winter break refining the floor plan, adjusting the window placements to maximize natural light. The reading nooks were tucked into corners, each one slightly different in size and shape.

The classroom smelled like wood glue and pencil shavings. Late morning light filtered through tall windows, catching dust motes that drifted through the air.

Other students filtered in gradually. Marcus arrived around 9:52, coffee cup in hand, looking half-awake. He dropped into his usual seat two rows over.

"Did you do the reading?"

Nico didn't look up from his sketch. "What reading?"

"Exactly." Marcus slumped forward, head resting on his folded arms.

Sarah and Mike came in together, mid-argument about some movie they'd seen over break. Their voices carried across the room—too loud, too energetic for a Wednesday morning.

"—and I'm telling you, the ending made no sense—"

"It made perfect sense, you just weren't paying attention."

Nico kept his head down, pencil moving across the page. The south-facing wall still needed work. He added annotations about the window materials.

Mr. Peterson arrived at 9:58, juggling a travel mug that said "World's Okayest Teacher" and a stack of papers. He set everything on his desk, took a long sip of coffee, and glanced at the clock.

"All right, settle down. Welcome back. Hope you all had restful breaks because this semester, we're diving into community design."

10:02. The door opened behind him.

"Ah, perfect timing. Everyone, this is Emma, transferring from Oakland." He gestured vaguely toward the back of the room. "Emma, grab any open seat. We're just getting started."

"As I was saying," Mr. Peterson continued, "community design is about understanding how people interact with space. Not just individual buildings, but the connections between them. The negative space. The pathways. What draws people together versus what keeps them apart..."

Nico's pencil kept moving. He'd heard this speech before; Mr. Peterson gave some version of it every semester. Around the room, other students were settling into mid-morning lethargy. Marcus had his head propped on his hand, fighting sleep. Mike was already on his phone under his desk, not even pretending to pay attention.

"...and this semester, we'll be working in pairs. I want you to design a community space, but the key is considering how people will actually use it..."

The scratch of pencils on paper. The hum of the heater. Someone's phone buzzing repeatedly. Mr. Peterson's voice washed over everything like background noise. Nico finished the south-facing wall of his library, started working on the east side. He'd need to adjust the roof pitch to account for—

A pencil rolled past his foot.

He glanced down. Wooden, yellow, slightly chewed at the eraser end. It kept rolling, hit the leg of his desk, and stopped. Nico bent down to pick it up. When he straightened, there was a girl standing at his desk.

The new one. Emma. She smiled slightly, reached for the pencil. "Thanks."

"You should get one of those weighted pencils," he said. "They don't roll."

"Oh. okay?"

He handed it over. She went back to her desk, next to Marcus, apparently. Nico returned to his sketchbook. The window on the east wall still needed work.

Friday, same week. 10 AM.

Nico arrived at 9:52. The class settled into its rhythm. Mr. Peterson assigned the main project: design a community space. Work in pairs. The rest of the period was for brainstorming.

The room erupted. Chairs scraping. People calling across the room to their usual partners.

"Want to work together?" Marcus asked Emma.

"Sure." They pulled their desks together. Started talking through ideas. Emma sketched while Marcus worked through the practical stuff, zoning requirements, and building codes.

Nico worked alone; he preferred it that way, no compromising on vision, no explaining his process. Just him and the design. The period passed. The bell rang at 10:50.

Nico packed up slowly, letting the crowd thin before heading to his next class.

The following weeks. Mid-February.

The following weeks blurred into routine. And then it was mid-February, Wednesday.

Mr. Peterson circulated the room while everyone worked on their projects. He stopped at Nico's desk.

"This is coming along nicely, Andretti. Very precise." He tapped the drawing. "But I want you to think about the emotional experience. How will people feel when they walk in? What will draw them to stay?"

Mr. Peterson moved on to the next desk, Marcus and Emma.

"Now this," he said, pointing at Emma's sketch, "captures feeling. But we need to make sure the physics actually work. Marcus, help her with the load calculations." Nico glanced over. Emma's design was loose, flowing, conceptually brilliant.

She caught him looking. Their eyes met for half a second. He looked away first.

Another Wednesday. 10 AM.

Emma's pencil rolled off her desk again.

This time, Nico saw it happen; her elbow bumped it while she was erasing something, and it tumbled off the edge. Rolled across the floor. Straight toward his desk.

He bent down, picked it up, and held it out to her.

"Here," he said.

"Thanks. Can I see what you're working on?"

"Sure," he said. She pulled up a chair. Sat close enough that he could smell her shampoo—something fruity, maybe apple? She studied his library design in silence for a long moment.

"It's really good," she said finally. "But it's kind of... lonely."

"What?"

"The library. All those windows looking out, but nowhere to gather. Like you're designing it for one person." Nico looked at his sketch with new eyes. She was right. Every sight line pointed outward. No communal spaces. No gathering points. Just individual alcoves and isolated reading nooks.

Marcus called over: "Emma, did you finish that elevation drawing?"

"Almost." She glanced back at Nico. "Thanks for the pencil."

"No problem."

She went back to her desk.

Nico stared at his library…his isolated, lonely library…and wondered what she saw when she looked at him.

That evening, the Andretti house smelled like garlic and onions. Laura was cooking—one of the rare nights when she had the energy to do more than order takeout. Nico could hear the sizzle of something in a pan, the clatter of a wooden spoon against cast iron.

He dropped his backpack by the door and headed for the stairs.

"Nico?" Laura called from the kitchen. "Dinner in twenty minutes."

"Okay."

In his room, he sat at his desk and stared at the wall. Downstairs, he could hear Luca arrive—the front door opening, keys hitting the bowl on the entry table, his father's voice: "Sorry I'm late. Got caught up."

Laura's response was too quiet to hear, but the tone carried. Snappy. Tight.

"I said I was sorry." More quiet words. "Laura, I can't just drop everything—"

"I'm not asking you to drop everything. I'm asking you to show up."

A knock on his door—light, familiar. "It's me," Gianna said.

"Come in." She entered and immediately flopped onto his bed as she owned it. "They're at it again."

"I heard."

"Dad's working on that development story. The wetlands thing. Mom thinks he cares more about that than us." Gianna sighed. "Want to watch something?"

"Yeah."

She pulled out her phone, queued up a show they'd been binge-watching—something mindless and comforting, a sitcom where problems were resolved in twenty minutes, and everyone always forgave each other. They'd seen most of the episodes already, but it didn't matter.

Downstairs, their parents' voices rang back and forth. Gianna turned the volume up.

Twenty minutes later, Laura called them down for dinner. Luca sat at the table, laptop closed but still in front of him like a barrier. Laura set plates down, and Gianna and Nico took their usual seats. For a few minutes, it was just the sound of silverware and chewing. Then Luca tried: "How was everyone's day?"

"Fine," Gianna said.

"Good," Laura said. Nico said nothing. Luca looked at him. "Nico? How's school?"

"Fine."

"Just fine?"

Nico shrugged. What was he supposed to say? That he sat alone in every class? He was counting down days until graduation? "He's taking architecture," Gianna offered. "He's good at it."

"That's great," Luca said, brightening. "What kind of projects are you working on?"

"Just... designs. Community spaces."

"What kind of community spaces?"

"I don't know yet. We just started." Laura stood abruptly. "I forgot to get drinks. Does anyone want water?"

Luca offered an unnatural, “Yes, please, honey.” He looked back at Nico. There was something in his expression—concern, maybe. Or guilt. "You doing okay, kiddo?"

"I'm fine."

"You sure?"

"Yeah."

Luca didn't look convinced, but he didn't push. Laura returned with water glasses, set them down, and sat. The meal continued in uncomfortable silence until Gianna started talking about her debate team, filling the void the way she always did. She was good at keeping conversations afloat.

After dinner, he went back to his room and pulled up college applications he'd started months ago. Filled in a few more lines. Stared at the essay prompts.

Describe a challenge you've overcome.

His stomach dropped, but it resolved quickly, and he let out a chuckle before he closed the laptop.
