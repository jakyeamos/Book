Left alone.

Age 13 - Sacramento.

Luca got a new job in Sacramento the family decided to move so he could chase bigger stories. Leaving Santa Fe was complicated, a tangle of relief and grief that sat like a stone in his chest. The night before the move, he sat on the floor of his empty room, tracing the faint pencil marks measuring his growth on the doorframe.

The drive was quiet and long, long silences briefly interrupted by requests for food or to use the bathroom. The new house was bigger but colder. His bedroom smelled like fresh paint and emptiness. He missed the quirks of the old house: the sticking closet door, the uneven floorboard that creaked when you stepped just right. Here, everything was flawless. Unwelcoming.

The school was worse. Names and faces blurred. He moved through the days aimlessly. He did well, a straight-A student, not because that was his goal, but it was just a result of his routine, burying himself in books to avoid home. At lunch, kids would invite him to sit with them. Sometimes he'd say yes. Make jokes. But it never felt right, so he'd find reasons to pull away. Sit alone. Reset to what felt more comfortable.

He finished the year with the highest grades in his class, and his teacher asked him to speak at the 8th-grade graduation. He didn’t really want to, but he also didn't care enough to argue. So the day came, His gaze scanned the audience, detached, until he found them, his family. His dad stood in the aisle, holding his expensive camera, grinning like he was the only person there. "That’s my kid!" Luca told anyone who’d listen, pride radiating from him like heat from the pavement. Gianna, face flushed with embarrassment, tugged at his arm, hissing, "Dad, sit down!"

Nico’s fingers tightened around the sides of the podium. The speech sat in his pocket, words printed neatly by his teacher. He unfolded the paper and set it down, ready to begin. He opened his mouth. Nothing came out. The auditorium buzzed, chairs creaking, a cough echoing from somewhere in the back. His pulse hammered. Noise swelled—rustling programs, shifting feet, the tap-tap-tap of someone’s pen—but it was distant, like hearing underwater. The lights blurred into streaks. His chest constricted.

Breathe. He couldn’t.

The crowd warped into a smear of colors. He squeezed his eyes shut, nails biting into his palms.

“Breathe,” The command came from somewhere inside his skull—sharp, cold, not quite his own voice but not quite separate either.

“Say something. Hurry up.”

Nico turned around to his left and then his right, searching for the origin of the voice, not finding one, he took a deep breath. His vision returned, his breathing slowed, and the words on the paper became legible. He finished the speech and walked off the stage. His dad stood at the bottom of the stairs, waiting.

“ ‘Colo, what happened up there? Are you ok?” Luca asked. Nico didn’t know what had happened. Was he okay? He decided against telling his dad about the voice.

“I don't know, just a little stage fright, but I took some deep breaths and got over it,” Nico said.

Age 15.

Two years in, and nothing felt like home. Luca's new job demanded more of him; his trips continued to grow in length. Laura's bitterness, once sharp and fiery, simmered into something colder and more harmful. Words between them felt like knives dulled from overuse but no less painful. Sometimes, it was more moments that left bruises hidden under sleeves. Gianna threw herself into school clubs and friendships, finding places Nico couldn't follow. Her calendar filled with color-coded activities, debate team, Spanish club, volunteer work at the animal shelter, weekend study groups that stretched into sleepovers. His stayed blank.

No one sat with him at lunch. He'd find a table in the corner, pull out a book he wasn't reading, and wait for the period to end. His grades, once solid, crumbled. Teachers called home. Laura's anger flared white hot. "Do you even care anymore?" she snapped after yet another parent email.

Nico shrugged. No. Not really.

Luca tried to step in, offering quiet encouragement, which only made it worse. She looked at Nico, "You're just like him, always with your head in the clouds, thinking you're too good for any of this." She said while pointing at Luca.

Nico started to get panic attacks pretty frequently. Breath caught in his throat. Chest clenching. World narrowing until it felt like he was being squeezed into nothingness. And then the fixations, a pencil out of place, footsteps that didn't land evenly. He'd find himself retracing steps, checking doors three times, aligning books on his desk until the tension in his chest eased just enough. Nico spent a lot of time on the roof staring at nothing in particular, sometimes hoping an airplane or a meteor would come to end the arguments. Sometimes Gianna would climb onto the roof beside him, breath clouding in the cold.

"You can't keep... floating like this," she said, tucking a blanket around her knees. "You should join the debate team with me, something to hold your attention."

She was his lifeline. That day, he found a flash drive with a note: Use in case of emergency—For My Favorite Brother (Not That You Have Competition). A playlist of songs they'd listen to together. She was great at the little things.

But Nico hated to burden her; his problems were his, he didn’t want to make her life any harder. One day, he snapped: "I'm fine. I don't need anyone to be here for me. I don't need anyone to help."

He saw his little sister's face drop immediately, and regretted the words as soon as they left his mouth.

"Maybe not," she said quietly. "But I always will." She climbed back through the window and shut his door on her way out.
