STATIC BETWEEN STATIONS.

Present Day

His phone buzzes. Nico opens his eyes, and he’s back on the couch. He sighs. Gianna’s still looking at him for acknowledgement of her request to talk to their mom. Loaf is sprawled across her lap like a melted puddle of orange fur. The TV played something neither of them was really watching—a sitcom laugh track echoing through the room, too bright, too fake.

His phone lights up again. Mom: Call me. He shows Gianna the message.

“Please. She’s alone. Just talk to her.”

Nico rubbed at his face, exhaustion threading into every breath. His brain played the greatest hits—Luca’s laugh echoing from old videos, the funeral haze, Laura’s biting words, Gianna’s tearful voice begging him to come home. The weight in his chest didn’t let up. It never does. “Not yet, Ana, I can’t,” he says, his body remembering every hit, his mind remembering each insult.

Gianna reaches over, grabbing his phone before he can shove it aside. “You can’t keep doing this.”

“Doing what?” His voice was flat. Brittle.

“Pretending it’s not eating you alive.” Her eyes—those same ones she had when they were kids—held him there. “You think you’re the only one hurting?”

“Here we go, Family intervention time.”

Nico stared off for a moment, long enough for the ever-observant Gianna to notice.

“Where do you go, you’re always looking as if there’s someone there or you’re waiting for something.” Half right. “It’s nothing,” he says. Another lie for the pile.

“She’s not perfect,” she said quietly, “but she’s still Mom. And she’s alone. We all are.” Her voice cracked. “You don’t have to talk to her for her. Do it for me. Or for Dad.”

His phone buzzed again. Mom: Please.

Gianna wiped at her eyes with her sleeve. “Just call her.”

The thought of hearing Laura’s voice again—sharp edges wrapped in guilt—made his stomach twist. But Gianna’s gaze didn’t waver.

“I’ll do it,” he muttered, “but only if you talk too.”

Gianna blinked, surprised, then nodded. “Really? Yeah. Okay. Of course.”

She tapped the screen and hit the speaker button. The rings are louder than he remembered, each one hanging in the air.

Click.

“…Hello?” Laura’s voice came through, wary. On edge. “Nicolo?”

“Yeah. It’s me mom,” Nico said. His voice cracked, “Gianna’s here too.”

A beat of silence stretched too long. Then, “You finally decided to answer,” Laura muttered, the relief undercut by bitterness.

Nico swallowed hard. “Didn’t think there was much to say.”

“That’s never stopped you before,” Laura shot back, knee-jerk sharpness out before she could rein it in. She sighed. Gianna cut in before the tension snapped taut. “We’re not calling to fight, okay? Just... talk.” Another pause. How long had it been since they had “just talked”? Did any of them even know how to do that anymore?

"It's been hard. Without him." Laura's voice was quiet. Vulnerable. "I just... I don't want us to fall apart completely."

"Bit late for that," Nico said, but the bite had dulled.

Laura let out a breathy laugh, tinged with sadness. "Maybe. But I'm still your mother. Doesn't that count for something?"

Gianna glanced at him. He didn't respond. "We don't hate you," Gianna said quietly.

"I'm making dinner tomorrow," Laura said. "You should come. Both of you."

"We'll think about it," Gianna said. "Okay."

Laura breathed. "That's all I can ask."

The call ended, but the weight lingered. Gianna lowered the phone, exhaling like she’d been holding her breath the whole time. They sat in silence for a minute, then two, almost three, before Gianna found another topic to bring up. “Have you been talking to anybody?”

Nico rubbed the back of his neck. “Not really.”

That wasn’t entirely true. Emma had texted him months ago—unexpected, a ghost resurfacing from a part of his life he’d thought was closed. Her family had moved when they were seniors, so she hadn’t heard about his dad until almost a year and a half after it happened. The first message was simple: Hey... just found out about your dad. I’m so sorry. I know it’s late, but I wanted to check in.

He’d almost ignored it. But something about the timing—or maybe just the loneliness—made him reply. What started as a one-off condolence turned into something else. Nothing serious, just... routine. Part of the background noise of his days. Similar questions every time: How’s work? How’s the club? How’s school? I miss you. We should meet up. Are you okay? How can I help?

He never really answered the last two. Didn’t know how. But replying was easier than not, and Emma never pushed when he changed the subject. Sometimes her texts made things feel lighter, like maybe not everything was falling apart. Other times, they reminded him of everything he’d lost. Of what he’d let slip away.

And then, like his brain flipping through tabs, it couldn’t close—Xander. Why the hell was he thinking about Xander? They weren’t friends. Barely talked. Just a guy from the library with a book always in hand and eyes that seemed to see through things. Their conversations, if you could call them that, were brief. Forgettable. Except... they weren’t. Not really. Xander’s presence lingered like a song played while doing homework—just enough to be distracting, not enough to pin down.

His phone buzzed again as if summoned by the thought. Emma’s name lit up the screen.

Gianna’s gaze dropped to his phone. Her expression shifted—recognition, curiosity, something else he couldn’t quite place. “Emma?”

He sighed. “It’s nothing.”

“Doesn’t look like nothing.”

“It’s just... we talk sometimes,” he muttered, swiping the notification away. “It’s not a big deal.”

Gianna arched a brow. “Really? Because I miss you, how are you holding up sounds like something.”

“She reached out after she found out about Dad. I answered. That’s all.” He mumbled.

Gianna didn’t speak for a moment, studying him in that way that always made him feel like she was reading a book he didn’t know he’d written. “You never really talked about her after you guys broke up.”

“Not much to talk about.”

“Except she’s still talking to you,” Gianna pointed out. Her tone wasn’t judgmental—just observant. “And you’re talking back.”

He exhaled, running a hand through his hair. “It’s easier than not.”

Gianna nodded slowly, then glanced at the phone again. “So... are you gonna reply?”

“Not right now.”

“Okay,” she said, leaning back. “But don’t act like you’re alone in this when people are reaching out. Even if it’s her.”

Nico leaned back, folding his arms. “Well... what about you? You talking to anybody?”

Gianna blinked, caught off guard. “Me?”

“Yeah.” He gestured vaguely. “You always have people around. Figured someone’s stuck.”

She huffed a soft laugh. “Not really.” Her gaze dropped to Loaf, absently running fingers through the cat’s fur. “Dated a guy last year—Ethan. Sweet, but clingy. Wanted to know where I was every second, freaked out if I didn’t text back right away. Too much.” She paused, lips giving way to a half-smile. “Ended that before it got worse.”

Nico nodded. “Sounds annoying.”

“Yeah, well... after that, just a few dates here and there. Nothing serious.” Her expression softened, eyes flicking back to him. “Hard to let people in when—” She broke off, shaking her head. “You know.” He did.

“There’s this one girl from my econ class,” Gianna added after a beat. “We grab coffee sometimes. I don’t know. She’s nice. But...” She shrugged again. “I don’t exactly have the bandwidth for, like, romance right now.”

“Yeah,” Nico related. They kept talking—topics ping-ponging between childhood memories, half-baked plans for the future, and random tangents that didn’t matter but filled the space anyway. It had been a while since a conversation felt this easy. Eventually, Gianna’s eyelids grew heavy, her words trailing into mumbles.

“What are you doing tomorrow?” Nico asked quietly. “Coming with me to work? Betsy asks about you sometimes.”

Half-asleep, she blinked, and for a second her eyes lit up at the mention of Betsy—the elderly librarian Nico had spent over a year trying to figure out, only for Gianna to charm her within minutes of meeting. “Yeah,” she mumbled, a smile tugging at her lips. “But you go too early... I’ll just meet you there.”

Her sentence slurred into the end of a breath as she tilted back, falling asleep mid-response. Nico sighed, grabbed the blanket from the back of the couch, and draped it over her. On his way to the bathroom, he reached into the cabinet, opened a still-packaged purple toothbrush, and placed it in the cup next to his own.

The apartment was still scattered with the aftermath of their night—takeout containers, her shoes kicked off by the door, a sweater draped over the armchair. He moved through the room with breakneck efficiency, clearing the clutter, straightening the cushions, wiping down surfaces until everything sat where it belonged. Gianna didn’t have the same need for cleanliness as her brother. By the time he was done, exhaustion had settled in his bones like concrete. He spared one last glance at the couch—Gianna breathing steadily under the blanket, Loaf curled at her feet—and turned toward his room. The door clicked shut behind him, sleep dragging him under fast.

Gianna stirred at the faint sounds of movement—the soft creak of floorboards, the rustle of fabric, keys jingling by the door. Blinking slowly, she lifted her head just enough to see Nico slipping on his jacket, tote bag already slung over his shoulder. His movements were efficient, practiced. Routine. A glance at the microwave clock on the counter made her groan. 6:10 a.m.

“How do you live like this?” she mumbled, voice thick with sleep. “It’s barely morning.”

Nico glanced over. “Early bird gets the quiet.”

“Early bird gets no sleep,” she shot back, dropping her head onto the pillow. “You’re so weird.”

“Text me when you head over,” he said, already reaching for the door.

“Mm-hmm,” she mumbled, eyes fluttering shut as the door clicked behind him. Footsteps faded down the hall, and for a moment, the apartment was blissfully still.

She meant to get up. Really. But the couch was warm, Loaf had reoccupied her lap, and the blanket was too cozy to abandon. Her body pulled her back under, the quiet settling like a weighted blanket. When she woke again, sunlight filtered through the curtains, and Loaf stretched against her leg. She fumbled for her phone—8:02 a.m.—and groaned. Definitely overslept. Dragging herself upright, she stretched until her joints popped, blinking away the remnants of sleep. The apartment still smelled faintly of coffee—evidence of Nico’s earlier presence—but was otherwise empty. His note sat on the counter in his familiar scrawl. Don’t be late. She giggled, finding it funny that her brother thought she was going to be on time.

She poured herself a cup of coffee, loading it with enough sugar to qualify it as dessert, and sipped as she shuffled to the bathroom. The purple toothbrush Nico had left her stood in the cup by the sink—a small thing that made her smile. You softie, she thought. Her phone buzzed. A text. She glanced down and winced when she saw the name.

Emma: Let me know if he’s okay. Haven’t heard back since yesterday.

Gianna exhaled through her nose, dragging a hand through her hair. Right. That. It hadn’t exactly been an easy decision, reaching out to Emma months ago. But watching Nico spiral and shut down had pushed her past hesitation. He needed someone. And Emma... well, she’d been that person once. Even if Nico didn’t want to admit it. Their messages had started off simple—Hey, you don’t know me well, but I’m Nico’s sister... He’s not doing great. Maybe you could reach out?—and, surprisingly, Emma hadn’t hesitated. Her texts to Nico had picked up from there—gentle check-ins, simple how-are-yous that Nico played off like they were no big deal. But Gianna knew better. He read each one, even if he didn’t answer them all.

She typed back: He’s fine. Same as always. I’ll keep you posted. She paused, then added, Thanks for trying.

Emma: Of course. Just... tell him I said hi.

Gianna rolled her eyes. She wasn’t the biggest fan of Emma; she had seen how she abandoned her brother before. But it was a necessary evil. Yeah, I will. She texted back. She wouldn’t.

After a quick rinse and pulling her hair into a loose bun, she grabbed her bag, tugged on her hoodie, and stepped into the cold morning air. Her breath puffed white against the chill, the streets just starting to wake up. She stopped by the café down the block, grabbing three pastries. The library wasn’t far. Its brick facade loomed, warm light spilling from the windows. She pushed open the door at 9:15, the creak echoing through the quiet. Warmth wrapped around her instantly—bookshelves stretching out like familiar arms, the soft hum of computers waking up filling the space.

“Gianna?”

Her head turned. Betsy stood near the returns desk, arms crossed but mouth tugging into what, for her, passed as a smile. “How have you been?! I haven’t seen you in ages.”

“Surprise,” Gianna grinned, holding up one of the pastries. “I come bearing gifts.”

“You’re much more charming than your brother,” Betsy says as she greets her with a hug. “Where is Nico?” Gianna responds.

“He has been reorganizing the entire Sci-Fi section since he got here. It looks like a crime scene.” Betsy slips before she can catch herself.

“I’m sorry, sweetie, I didn’t mean—”

“It’s okay, Betsy, I promise,” Gianna responded with a very convincing fake smile crafted with years of practice. Nico turns the corner into view as if summoned by even an indirect mention of his father.

“Hey. You look like you’ve been here for hours.” She says the eyebags under Nico’s eyes are somehow even more evident than usual.

“I have,” he said dryly. “You’re late.”

“Fashionably,” she shot back, dropping her bag on the counter. “Besides, I brought you something.” She held out a pastry bag like an offering.

“Apricot?” He asks. She smirks and nods with confirmation. Nico grabs the bag and begins to nibble, doing a barely noticeable shimmy after each bite. “Fanks,” he says with a mouth full of food.

Gianna glanced at her phone again, another text from Emma lighting up the screen: Any updates? How’s he doing?

Her smile dimmed but didn’t fade. She typed back: He’s... being Nico. But he’s okay.

Emma replied almost instantly. Thanks. Seriously.

Pocketing her phone, Gianna glanced at her brother.

“Class today?”

“Yeah,” Nico said. “12:45.”

She sipped her drink. “So you just... come to work at the crack of dawn for fun?”

“Beats lying awake doing nothing.” His tone was matter-of-fact, not self-pitying. Just the truth. “Shouldn’t you have class today? ”

“Meh, one day won’t hurt,” She offered as a response. “My grades were better than yours in high school. You can’t lecture me.” Nico pauses his bite and opens his mouth to argue, but he can’t. While his grades dropped like rocks, garnering attention from his parents that he craved, Gianna stayed focused and diligent. She wasn’t an all-A student, but she maintained a very respectable GPA in her honors-loaded schedule.

“Whatever, just don't make it a habit.” He barked before diving back into his Danish.

Gianna circled the counter and dropped into the chair next to Nico, her coffee cup thumping against the desk as she settled in. “God, my feet already hurt,” she muttered, stretching her legs out. Her gaze drifts toward the entrance, where patrons trickle in and out under the flickering overhead light. A familiar smirk crept across her face. “Hey... you remember that game we used to play?”

Nico didn’t look up from the computer. “Nope.”

“Liar.” She nudged his arm. “Come on. Making up stories? You always acted like you hated it, but you were better at it than me.”

He sighed but didn’t protest when she propped her chin on her hand and scanned the room like she was casting a movie. “Oh, okay, look—guy with the briefcase.” She nodded toward a man in a rumpled suit, checking his phone near the reference shelves. “Bet he’s a failed novelist working a nine-to-five he hates. Comes here to pretend he’s researching for a book, but really he’s just avoiding his desk job.”

“Or,” Nico murmured without even glancing up, “he’s an international super spy regretting every life choice that led him to this point.”

“See?” Gianna grinned. “You do remember.”

They fell into the old rhythm easily; it was muscle memory. It was a game that recalled childhood afternoons spent on park benches, mall food courts, or anywhere their mom dragged them when they were too young to be left alone.

“That guy—” Gianna nodded at a teenager in a leather jacket loitering near the magazine rack. “Total rebel with a tragic backstory. Definitely writes bad poetry and broods on rooftops.” Nico laughed a little because that described him in high school.

“He looks like he stole that jacket from someone cooler,” Nico said. “Probably thinks cigarettes make him mysterious.”

Gianna laughed. A woman in business attire breezed past them, earbuds in, heels clicking sharply against the floor. “She’s on her way to fire someone,” Gianna decided. “But she’ll cry about it later because deep down she just wants to be loved.”

“Is this a Hallmark movie? She’s just late for a meeting,” Nico countered. Then Gianna’s gaze snagged on someone new. “Oh, she’s pretty,” she said, dropping her voice as a girl stepped through the entrance—mid-twenties, dark hair ending around her chin, scarf wrapped around her neck. Her steps were measured and deliberate, like she knew exactly where she was headed. “I bet she’s an artist. Or a poet. Moved here for a fresh start after a messy breakup.” Gianna grinned. “Probably writes her number on napkins and leaves them in coffee shops.”

Nico wasn’t listening anymore.

His eyes had locked on the girl, something in his chest hitching. Why does she look so familiar? His brain scrambled, flipping through memories, details half-formed—until she stopped by the philosophy section. Her fingers skimmed along the spines like she was searching for something specific. And then she pulled out The Myth of Sisyphus.

It clicked—the girl who wrote in the margins.

“Hello? Earth to ‘Colo, you there?” Gianna followed his gaze, which had been tracing the movements of the lady. “Ohhh. You’re checking her out.”

"What? No---" He tried to deflect, but Gianna was already nudging his arm, grinning like she'd caught him in something scandalous.

"Look at you---haven't seen you make that face since... God, I don't know when." Her grin widened. "Should I go talk to her for you? Get her number?" She glanced back at the woman.

“You should at least go talk to her."

"It's not like that," Nico muttered. And it wasn't. He'd just been... looking. The way people look at strangers sometimes, wondering about their lives. Sonder, his dad used to call it. The recognition that everyone has a story as complex as your own. Before he could say anything else, his phone buzzed and rang---his alarm.

Fuck. 12:30.

He cursed under his breath, slinging his backpack over his shoulder. With a quick wave to Betsy, he pivoted toward his sister. Gianna stood waiting, arms crossed but smiling. He wrapped her in a hug. “Go to class, Ana,” he ordered. She rolled her eyes, smirking. “I’ll see you back at your apartment—we’re taking your car to dinner.” Dinner? His brain scrambled—what dinner? Then the memory surfaced from last night’s haze: plans he hadn’t thought would come to fruition. His face twisted into a grimace as he opened his mouth to protest. Gianna’s head tilted, gaze sharp and unwavering, locking him in place. She didn’t speak. She didn’t need to. Her look was a challenge in itself—a silent dare to backpedal. He sighed, shoulders sinking. A quiet, “Yeah,” was all he could muster.

12:43. He slipped into his usual seat—third row, second from the left—just as the professor queued up the lecture slides. The room buzzed with quiet conversation, the soft shuffle of notebooks opening, laptops clicking, and the occasional hum of someone's playlist leaking through earbuds. "Yo, Nico, you get number seven on the homework?" He turned. Marcus leaned over from the next seat. "Yeah, I got like... 42?" "Same. Cool." They got along fine—talked about weekend plans they never invited each other to, traded notes, made easy conversation about nothing important. Class friends.

Across the room sat Grace—once one of his closest friends, sometimes more than that. Things between them weren't awkward; they still exchanged the familiar nods. Occasionally, she'd text him for help with homework, her messages punctuated with the same casual *"thanks!"*

He remembered sitting on his dorm room floor at 2 am, shaking, tears soaking into the sweatshirt he never wore again. Her hand on his back. "Breathe, Nico. Just breathe." She'd stayed through the worst of it—the panic attacks, the sleepless manic stretches, the days after his father died when getting out of bed felt impossible. She didn't flinch. She just stayed.

Now it was a nod across the room. A half-smile. A "thanks!" in a text.

The professor’s voice cut through his thoughts. “All right, everyone. Let's dive into spatial theory and human interaction with architectural environments—midterms are coming, so take notes.” Notebooks opened around him, and laptops clicked awake. He pulled out his mechanical pencil, the weight of the graphite familiar and comforting. On the screen flashed slides of urban landscapes, diagrams of circulation paths, and buildings designed to evoke emotion. Space as experience, the title read.
