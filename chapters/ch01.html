Sunday, March 8th, 2036
Olympia, Washington

THE RITUAL.

Xander

A cold sweat clings to his forehead.

A headache pulsates behind his eyes.

He Stretches…

Hold,

Shiver,

Release.

His fingers skim across the bedside table, searching, too dazed to sit up just yet. After a few passes, he finds his phone. A faint glow emerges as it recognizes his face, and a slight click signifies its unlocking. Swipe. Tap. Scroll. He moves with purpose, aiming to break the horrid silence of the room, his phone connected to an Alexa on his desk. With another flick of his thumb, the first few notes of a song disrupt the silence. The sound trying, and failing to bury the overwhelming thoughts that accompanied his mornings.

An unfocused gaze maps the room. Searching for nothing in particular. It's the SAME. The SAME walls. The SAME dull hum of the fan. The SAME list of things he was meant to do yesterday, last week, and last year.

Even without the clarity of his glasses, familiarity sharpens the edges of his morning. He shakes off the weight of sleep, and sits up, and lets his mind wander:

Thinking about how much he had changed in the last few years. So many different thoughts, actions, people, relationships. So many different Xanders.

Each felt final, real, but somehow, he outlived them all. The room, witness to this, remains the same, unimpressed by his existence.

He turns to the window. The city was silent, covered in grey haze. The skyline and everything beneath it blanketed—every soul, every stone, every leaf. The buildings stand unbothered, unaffected by the darkness of the sky, which seems poisonous to everything else.

He walked past a growing pile of laundry, each sock and shirt outlasting his desire for order.

"Why do I have so many clothes?" Such a thinly veiled cover for his insecurities. Insecurities that he could name, dissect, and understand, but not escape.

His mind wanders to a girl he used to date, one who left her coding job to pursue fashion in New York. Trading syntax for silk, and clean-cut algorithms for a messy artistry of eating disorders.

He told her it was a scam, that fashion was just a capitalist scheme, convincing people they were unfinished canvases, that the right cut, the right color, the right brand could make them whole. She just laughed. "It's more than surface-level grandeur; It's armor. Expression. Emotion."

They cycled through the same argument, over and over, like a scratched record stuck on the same refrain.

He never found the voice to tell her that he hoped she was right. Because now in present day, he stands in a room of evidence contrary to his own beliefs:

Hangers lined with leather,

shelves stacked with shirts,

jackets he bought for a version of himself that never arrived.

Shoes worn once, then swiftly abandoned when he realized they couldn't fix his posture or presence.

The manufactured desires. The industry tactics that turn self-doubt into profit margins. He understood it all and still fell victim.

He envied her. Not the fame, the bravery. She saw the risks and decided it was worth it anyway. She didn't talk herself out of wanting more. Now what is he left with? The certainty of this room. The hum of the fan. The window overlooking a sidewalk he knows too well.

A fantasy about how it would feel to step through the mesh covering the window. Letting gravity bear the weight of every choice he's ever made. The wind rushing up to meet him like an old friend. The ground, stern but fair—soft in its certainty.

And then—like always—the guilt comes crashing in.

These thoughts are not just indulgent but greedy. That in his worst moments, he can think only of his escape, while everyone else is left to carry the weight of it.

How arrogant.

How selfish.

He lets out a slow breath and moves to the bathroom.

The shower is hot, scalding, even; he doesn't turn the temperature down. He lets it sear against his skin, like it might burn something out. There's a switch to turn on the vent in the bathroom, and each morning, he pretends to be oblivious to that fact, letting the mirror fog over, obscuring a reflection he hates to acknowledge.

He goes through the motions with precision; each step is ingrained into his muscle memory. A cleanser, toner, lotion, sunscreen. He presses them into his skin with the same meticulous care an artist might give to a canvas, as if each step might erase something deeper than just the dullness and dryness of his skin.

By the time he is done, his skin is smooth, his hair damp and heavy against his shoulders. The mirror has cleared enough to show his reflection, but he doesn't look at it.

He moves over to the dresser, his fingers finding the handle in the same place it was yesterday. He reaches inside, pulling at a shirt he used to love, back when there was still an image to maintain. Now, it's just fabric, just another thing he puts on without thinking.

His fingers close around the collar, but before it pulls free, a sharp sting.

Heat spreads through his fingertip, and for a moment, he just watches. The pain is clean, almost elegant in its precision, a bright thread cutting through the numb haze of the morning. He takes in the puddle of red while it wells up, slow and deliberate, blooming like hibiscus. He squeezes his finger, coaxing out another bead, watching the surface tension hold until it finally breaks, sliding down toward his palm. He brings it to his lips. His tongue meets the copper tang, metallic and warm, something is grounding in its familiarity.

Proof that he's here. That his body still responds. That he is not just a collection of looping thoughts and half-formed memories, drifting through the weight of another morning. He always had respect for pain. Sharp and undeniable, pulling him into this exact moment with force. He learned that pain is like any other drug: its intensity fades, and its effects demand escalation.

He's known many forms, the sear of heat pressed too long against the skin, the quiet bite of nails tracing red lines into flesh, the tight ache behind the eyes when a breath is held past comfort, the cold leather of belts that wrap his breath as snugly as they hold up a pair of pants, just to see how long the body will fight back, and the scars, some fading, some stubborn, all reminders of a body that has learned pain like a second language. Little violences, self-contained, secretive.

He flexes his hand and feels the small, electric hum still clinging to his nerves. He finally looks down.

A bobby pin. Caught between his Jeff Buckley Grace t-shirt and the fake wood paneling of the dresser.

The shirt is soft, worn thin in places, fabric that holds the memory of every wash. He slides his arms into it and lets the cotton settle against his skin. Then the sweater, thicker, heavier, sleeves a little too long.

The jacket comes last. The weight of it presses down on his shoulders; He smooths his hands over the material, pressing out invisible creases.

He steps out into the hallway, the door clicking shut behind him. The hallway is empty, lined with identical doors, each concealing lives that barely intersect with his own. Somewhere down the hall, someone laughs—a short, sharp burst before disappearing into silence again. The sound is light, easy, and unburdened. It irritates him more than it should. He slips on his headphones.

The elevator arrives with a mechanical chime, the left door opening slightly slower than the right. He steps inside and watches the numbers descend, the flickering red digits marking his slow approach to the ground; a less exciting parallel to his morning fantasies. The air inside is thick with the remnants of someone's else's cologne, something citrusy and sharp, clashing against the metallic scent of the elevator itself. The doors open with a dull groan.

There's someone at the printer in the lobby, and he lowers his gaze. He knows they won't talk to him, but he avoids eye contact anyway. He continues out of the building, the security guard barely glances up from his phone. Outside, the city greets him with usual indifference. The walk to the library is familiar; his feet move without much thought. At the intersection, the pedestrian signal flicks to WALK.

The moment he steps inside, the familiar stillness settles over him. The smell— paper, ink, old glue —hasn't changed; a light layering of dust covers the books. He's smelled the inside of this building a thousand times, and somehow, that steadiness soothes him instead of making him feel stuck.

He nods to Nico, who works at the front desk.

Friend?

They've never hung out outside these walls, never even spoken beyond the boundaries of this library.

Acquaintance?

And yet, he knows his reading habits—the way he annotates in pencil, never pen, as if every thought is temporary. The way he lingers longer in philosophy than fiction, fingertips ghosting over the pages like he's trying to pull something tangible from the ink.

He has a presence that feels unintentional. Broad shoulders at ease, a posture suggesting he's constantly in deep thought. Dark curls that fall slightly out of place, that beg to be pushed back absentmindedly, though he never does. There's something patient in the way he moves, measured but never hesitant, like he's always waiting for something—a revelation, a perfect line, a moment worth holding onto.

He wonders if Nico's ever found it.

Beyond him, Betsy leans against the counter, muttering about an improperly filled-out return slip. The same spot she's stood every morning for—what, five years now? Six? She was here when he started. She'll probably be here when he leaves.

He lets his fingers drift along the worn spines of the books. The friction between his thumb and the rough cloth makes his stomach turn slightly, but he has learned to push past it. Eventually, he settles on a book he has read too many times.

In his usual corner, away from wandering eyes, he sets the book on its spine and lets it fall open to a random page.

He skims the text, then pauses—

The rehearsal.

I wear a leased laugh and a stolen smile

stretched to fit my face.

I walk as if I belong

following footsteps left by those before me.

They say fake it till you make it.

If you do something long enough, it becomes true.

I say sentences, practice paces, train tones, mold the shape of my mouth.

Does it ever stop being an act

An imitation of what seems right?

Or did I just forget

that it isn't real?
