The routine.

Nico

He wakes up and turns to look at the clock: 5:18 am. Twelve minutes till his alarm officially starts his day. He closes his eyes, trying to go back to sleep

—his attempts blocked by unapologetic brain noise thoughts bouncing through every part of his head.

After a few minutes, he gives up, and he twists to the left, then to the right; an assortment of cracks and pops ring out from his vertebrae.

Loaf, his slightly overweight orange tabby, stirs beside him, stretching long and slow before hopping off the bed. Nico pulls the sheets tight, smooths out each wrinkle, and tucks in the corners. The room is clean, stripped of anything unnecessary. No clothes draped over chairs. No stray books. No clutter. Tiny specks of litter Loaf has tracked across the floor catch his eye. He sweeps them up, then checks. No more. Good.

A book on his desk sits slightly misaligned, its spine not quite parallel to the others. His fingers twitch. He reaches for it and pushes it back into place.

Nico walks into the bathroom and flips the light switch. It doesn’t snap into place as a new one would. Age and wear have made it sluggish, forcing him to push it up, slow and deliberate, like shoving something through mud. He’s put in more maintenance requests than he can count, each one behind the last on the “non-emergency” waitlist. The light flickers on, then off, then on again, every morning. He takes a deep breath and resumes his routine as the light finally agrees to do its job.

He walks deeper into the bathroom, which is similarly spotless; he reaches for the temperature knobs of the shower. Three and a quarter turns for the one on the left, one and a quarter for the one on the right. The high-pitched shrill of the knobs rings through his ears. He keeps the water hot, just shy of burning, and steps in. One of the spouts on the shower head has stopped working. He stares at it. One out of fifty. Forty-nine working. His hand moves toward the tap. He stops. Breathes.

Shampoo. Rinse. Conditioner—leave it in for exactly two minutes. Wash his face, then his body. His hands move over his arms, his shoulders, his chest. He scrubs hard. Too hard. He knows it, but he doesn’t stop. Just a little longer. His fingers press into his skin, running over the same spot again, again, and again. The lather is gone, but he keeps scrubbing—as if there’s something still left to erase.

“You’re already clean.”

But it doesn’t feel that way. He presses harder, until the friction turns to heat, until his skin feels raw beneath his palms. He doesn’t stop. Not yet.

"Stop."

The word lands with weight. Not a voice exactly—a command from somewhere he can't pinpoint. His own? Something else? The distinction blurs under the molten downpour.

Nico exhales sharply and takes a step back, allowing the water to run over him. He turns the water off and steps out, towel wrapped around his waist, cool air biting at his skin.

He walks to the sink, his reflection stares back with stern green and brown eyes, his thin frame, and the sharp lines of his collarbone are prominent. His pale skin contrasts with the dark bags under his eyes and his eyelashes, which he thinks are a little too long for a boy. His damp hair clings to his forehead in uneven curls. He runs a hand through it, watching the strands separate and settle, a few refusing to lie flat. No matter how much he fixes it, it always looks a little out of place. He folds the towel he just used, places it on its hook, and aligns the edges. His hands move over a dresser of identical socks neatly folded, debating which pair to take before settling on one.

Back in the bedroom, Loaf is perched on the sill, seemingly arguing with a bird that frequents the other side of the window. Nico moves to his dresser, pulling out clothes with mechanical precision. A black T-shirt, soft with wear, and grey sweatpants.

After getting dressed, he turns to his desk, where his weekly pill organizer sits. The see-through compartments, each labeled with a day of the week, are all empty—except for today, the final compartment, Sunday. By tonight, he’ll need to refill the entire thing. He flips open the small tab, shaking the contents into his palm. A multivitamin, Magnesium, Ashwagandha, Omega-3s, and then the prescriptions. One for focus. One for mood, one that is supposed to quiet the noise. He swallows them one by one, the way he always does. Once the last pill is gone, he exhales and taps the empty compartment with his fingertip, snapping it shut.

Loaf looks with an empty stare, “Are you judging me?”, Nico says, Loaf flicks her tail, maybe an act of confirmation. He opens the door, steps through it, and closes it, rummaging through his pockets for the key and locking the door.

He walks down the hall, then turns back to check the door. Locked. He continues walking, then stops. Turns back again. Still locked.

The elevator arrives with a chime, he turns away, but his stomach still drops, knowing the left side will open slightly slower than the right. He steps through the frame and presses his thumb against the cool railing. The doors slide shut. The lobby is empty. The security guard there, not watching, just present, phone screen reflecting off his glasses.

Outside, it's warmer than it looks, uncharacteristic for a city caught in the limbo of night and morning. It's quiet, wind, trees rustling, the quiet hum of a car moving a few blocks away. As Nico crosses the street, his feet find the white stripes of the crosswalk. Each step is deliberate.

He arrives at the library, and the moment he steps inside, his demeanor changes, his pace slows, his hands escape their strict confinement. Betsy is already behind the counter, flipping through paperwork, her expression caught between boredom and irritation.

“You’re early,” she says without looking up.

“I know.”

His hands are cold. He flexes them, trying to shake the feeling. Betsy has on the radio, classical music ringing out softly;

Nico moves toward the philosophy section, letting his fingers graze the spines of books, noting their weight and their texture. He loves to read. Not for answers, not for comfort—just to see what other people have thought. The clock strikes 8 am, the start of his shift. He places Das Kapital back on the shelf and begins his tasks back at the front desk. Shortly after the library opens, a familiar figure enters: Xander. He offers a nod to Nico and then moves toward the mythology section.

A pair of students linger near the front desk, their voices low but carrying enough to catch snippets.

"—he’s here again," one says, half-laughing.
"I swear, he’s like a character in a movie or something."
"Yeah, I mean... look at him."

Nico doesn’t mean to listen to or indulge in their meaningless gossip. But his gaze drifts toward the corner where Xander sits, hunched over a book.

He gets it. Xander’s the kind of person people talk about. Tall, with shoulder-length hair that curls at the ends, caught somewhere between waves and tangles. His face is all sharp lines and clean angles, the kind of handsome that could be intimidating if he carried himself differently, but there’s no arrogance in him. Just... reserved. Someone content to let the world move around him while he stays put.

Xander is predictable in a way Nico finds oddly grounding. Same time. Same corner. Cycling through the same few books like he’s waiting for them to offer something new. Nico watches Xander grab a book he’s grabbed many times before, “The Myth of Sisyphus.” He carries it to his corner and sits.

He looks back at his work computer. He’s been coding a digital book return system. It’s 2036, and Betsy still has everything analog. What a stubborn old lady. But recently her hands hurt more frequently, and her vision has lost its range, so Nico works on his project secretly, hoping to surprise her at some point.

The remaining hours of his shift drag on, blurring into a haze of check-ins, check-outs, and monotonous shelf reordering. Finally, the clock hits 5 pm, Nico grabs his things, and heads for the door.

It’s raining, heavy raindrops play a pretty percussion along the ground. Usually after his Sunday shift, he heads to ______ club but today he’s just tired. He enters his apartment building, taking a second to appreciate the dryness and warmth. He bypasses the rickety elevator, taking the stairs two at a time. He reaches the 4th floor and pushes through the stairwell door. His gaze catches on the bulletin board across the hall, his face plastered among the other RAs. The grainy photograph stares back at him, the forced smile doing little to hide the truth: it’s a terrible job. Middle-of-the-night lockouts. Ambulance calls for students testing their limits with liquor. Endless, hollow meetings. The perks? Free housing. So he deals with it.

He turns the corner, stopping cold. His door, usually locked, is slightly ajar. Not wide open, just enough to assume someone swung the door closed, not checking to see if it shut all the way. A breath catches in his throat. His fingers drift into his jacket pocket, curling around the cool handle of a pocket knife. His pulse quickens, adrenaline sharpening every sound, the faint hum of distant plumbing, the electric buzz overhead, the rain tapping relentlessly at the window at the end of the hall.

“Why didn’t you check the door a third time? Stupid.” He thinks. As he reaches his room, he presses his ear to the door. Holds his breath. The world narrows. He hears Loaf meowing, high-pitched. His grip tightens on the knife, knuckles whitening. Heartbeat pounding in his ears, bassline steady and hard. He counts to three and opens the door. The living room comes into focus, and there, casually sprawled on his couch, playing with Loaf like she owns the place, his sister, Gianna. The tension drains from him, but not all at once. He exhales a shaky breath, slipping the knife back into his pocket before she notices.

She looks up mid-laugh, her face stretched with a contagious smile, holding a toy mouse that Loaf swats at with disinterested precision.

“Jesus, Nico, took you long enough,” she says. “I brought you some soup from home.”

It’s been months since they were in the same room. Her jet-black hair has grown longer, falling in sleek waves to the middle of her back. There’s maroon polish on her nails, chipped at the edges, and eyeliner frames her dark brown eyes, making them sharper. Gianna’s always been expressive, emotions loud, and unapologetic, whereas Nico folds his into corners, tucks them behind clenched jaws, and averted gazes. Her skin holds a naturally warmer tone, a subtle olive glow that contrasts with his paleness. Her features are softer and rounder. Her posture relaxed. She lounges with the kind of ease he’s never been able to replicate.

Her gaze drifts toward his cluttered desk. Files lie scattered—printouts of old emails, photos of construction sites, permit applications with his father's notes scrawled in the margins. Names circled. Question marks everywhere. Addresses. Dates that don't quite line up, or maybe do, depending on how you look at them.

Among them, a copy of the accident report sits partially open, stark black text bleeding into white paper. Her expression falters. The warmth she had cooled. "You're still doing this?" Her voice isn't accusatory, just tired. Worn down in a way that feels older than she is. She rubs a hand over her face, sighing. "Nico..."

"I know," he mutters, but he doesn't close the folder.

"Do you?" She glances at the desk again, at the hours he's poured into connecting dots that might not connect. "Because it looks like you're trying to turn an accident into a murder."

"I'm just—"

"You're making yourself crazy." Her voice softens. "And Mom's not wrong to be worried."

For a moment, she just stares, lips pressed together. Like she’s weighing whether to push or let it drop. Her fingers drum against the container of soup. Then, finally, she shakes her head. “I get it,” she says quietly. “Believe me, I do. Just... don’t lose yourself in it, okay? He wouldn’t have wanted that.”

Gianna sighs again but moves on quicker than she normally would. “Eat before it gets cold,” she mumbles.

Loaf climbs back into her lap as Gianna sits back down. Gianna scratches at the cat’s chin, earning a delighted purr, her bracelets clinking as she moves. Loaf’s loyalty is usually reserved for Nico alone, but Gianna has always been good at winning people, and apparently, cats over.

“You could’ve texted. You know I don't like surprises. How did you even get in here? I know I locked the door,” he deflects.

Gianna rolls her eyes, pulling out her phone, waving it like evidence. “I texted you… twice. You didn’t answer your messages, Nicolo Andretti.” His full name, she’s obviously not happy with him.

“I made a copy of your key last time you were home,” she reveals, shrugging. No apology, just fact. That’s how she’s always been, direct, infuriating, and endearing. He could be mad. Should be, maybe. But it’s Gianna. His little sister, chaotic, stubborn, and his best friend.

“Sit down,” she says instead, nodding toward the couch. “Our show’s on.”

Loaf abandons Gianna’s lap to trot toward Nico, tail high like a flag. She weaves between his legs, purring loud enough to fill the quiet pockets of the room. Nico scoops her up, feeling the familiar weight settle on his lap. He absently scratches behind her ear as he sits beside Gianna.

Gianna glances at him sideways, her gaze moving away from the TV. Finally, she pauses the show.

“So…” Her voice dips quieter as if treading on something fragile. “Have you talked to Mom lately?” The question lands heavily.

Nico’s shoulders tense. Have you talked to Mom lately? His phone might as well vibrate 1000 times to represent all the unread messages gathering dust in his inbox. “Hey, just checking in.” “When are you coming home?” “Call me.” Each one a pebble adding weight to his pocket.

“No,” he says finally, voice flat. Gianna sighs through her nose. Not surprised or even disappointed, she already knew the answer and the reasoning. She pulls her knees up to her chest, arms circling them. “Yeah. Thought so.” Her thumb skims over the remote but doesn’t press play.

“I’ve been calling her since I moved into college,” Gianna says. “Not every day, but… enough. She’s getting older, Nico. She needs someone.” Her gaze sharpens, searching his face. “She’s all alone.”

His jaw tightens. He traces a finger along the cat’s back.

“She made her choices.”

Gianna lets the silence stretch before speaking again—quieter, but edged with something that lingers between pleading and frustration. “Yeah, well… so did we.”

He leans back into the couch, head tipping toward the ceiling. Loaf stretches across his lap, paws kneading his thigh gently like she’s trying to start a bakery.
And behind closed eyes, it’s not darkness he sees—

It’s home.

It’s then.

The Ever-Absent Mercy of a Clock.

The clock repeats

Its hands sway and tick

saying what it has always said

Each hour, a bruise or a scar

I rise, I move, I make my bed

The world marches

It's a silent partnership with time

But today I am trapped in yesterday's shade

My life is not as simple as a line

Routine wraps tightly like a rope

Another dawn, a sky half fed

What does today hold

Something different, I hope

I rise, I move, I make my bed.

What changes each day

When the clock decides to tick and sway

saying what it has always said.

There's a fear in my chest.

It lingers heavy like lead.

The pattern won’t fray.

This labyrinth won’t lose to rest.

Yet and still

The clock repeats what it’s always said

And I rise,

I move,

I make my bed.
