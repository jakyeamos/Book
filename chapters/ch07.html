Tuesday.

ALL GOOD THINGS.

Nico

Tuesday passed in a blur of normalcy. Classes, lunch with Emma, dinner at home.

Between classes, he passed Trevor and Maya in the hallway. They were holding hands again, whatever fight they'd had on Monday already resolved. Trevor nodded at him. Nico nodded back.

After school, Nico went to the library. Actually studied. Not avoiding home, just doing homework like a normal person. Emma texted him memes throughout, and he found himself smiling at his phone more than once. At dinner, his family talked about their days. His dad told a story about his first story, where he had to report on flat-earthers. They all laughed, even his mom.

That night, lying in bed, Nico thought: This is what it could have been like all along. If his parents hadn't fought so much. If his dad had been around more. If he hadn't been so stuck in his own head. But they were trying now, all of them. Maybe it wasn't too late.

Thursday, 11:47 PM.

Nico was in bed, half-asleep. He'd finished his homework hours ago, texted Emma goodnight, and scrolled through social media until his eyes got heavy. His room was dark except for the faint glow of the streetlight outside filtering through his blinds.

His phone buzzed on the nightstand. Lit up.

Dad

He blinked, groggy, reached for it. "Hello?"

"Hey kiddo, did I wake you?" His dad's voice came through.

"It's fine. What's up?"

"Just wanted to tell you—I think I've got enough for the story. Finally."

Nico rolled onto his back, more awake now. "That's good."

"Yeah. It is. Got the last piece tonight. The interview went really well. Everything's coming together." A pause. "I know I've been distracted lately. With work. I'm sorry about that."

"It's okay."

"No, it's not. But I'm done now. All done." Another pause, longer. "We should celebrate this weekend. Pizza and that sci-fi movie you wanted to see? The one with the… time travel?"

Nico smiled in the dark. "Yeah. That sounds good."

"Saturday?"

"Saturday."

"Perfect." His dad's voice softened. "I'm proud of you, 'Colo. That building design? Mr. Peterson sent me photos. That's real talent. Don't forget that."

Something warm spread through Nico's chest. "Thanks, Dad."

"Love you, kiddo."

"Love you too."

"Get some sleep."

"Okay, see you soon."

Click.

2:14 AM.

BANG

BANG

BANG

Nico jolted awake, heart hammering, completely disoriented. The pounding continued—not knocking, something more desperate, more urgent.

"'Colo! 'Colo, wake up!"

Gianna's voice. Wrong. All wrong. He stumbled out of bed, legs tangling in sheets. Nearly fell. Caught himself on the dresser, pulse racing. The pounding didn't stop. He yanked the door open.

Gianna stood there in her pajamas, face blotchy and red, tears streaming down her cheeks. Her hands were shaking. Her breath came in gasps, hitching and uneven.

"What—" he started.

"Downstairs," she choked out. "Now."

His stomach dropped. "What happened?"

"Just come. Please."

He followed her down the stairs, bare feet on cold hardwood. Each step felt wrong, like walking through water, time stretching strange. The living room lights were all on—too bright, harsh, emergency-bright.

Two police officers stood near the couch. One woman, short with dark hair. One man, tall and uncomfortable-looking. They had that posture, that expression. Official. Grim.

Laura was on the couch. Not sitting. Collapsed. Her body folded in on itself, hands over her face.

The female officer saw him. Her face softening with practiced sympathy, the expression they teach you for delivering bad news.

"Are you Nicolo Andretti?"

Nico nodded. Couldn't speak. Throat closed.

"I'm Officer Martinez. This is Officer Patterson." She glanced at her partner, then back. "I'm very sorry to have to tell you this. There's been an accident."

The words came slow. Or maybe his brain moved slow.

"Your father's vehicle was involved in a single-car collision on Highway 50, approximately fifteen miles outside Sacramento." Highway 50. Single-car collision. The words were separate, floating, not connecting into a meaning.

"The road conditions were poor black ice. The vehicle left the roadway and struck a tree."

Black ice. Tree. His dad. Highway 50.

Still not connecting.

"Emergency services arrived within ten minutes. They did everything they could."

They did everything they could.

That's the sentence that means—

Nico heard the words. Understood them individually. Each one made sense on its own. Together, they didn't make sense at all.

Gianna's hand found his. Her fingers were ice-cold. Or maybe his were. He couldn't tell.

Laura's wailing crescendoed, filling the entire room, pressing against the walls, the ceiling, his ribs. Officer Patterson spoke: "We're so sorry for your loss." He was holding his hat in his hands, turning it. Uncomfortable. Wishing he were anywhere else.

Nico's hearing went strange. Underwater. Muffled. He could see Officer Martinez's mouth moving, but the sound was delayed, distorted, like watching a badly dubbed movie.

Two hours ago, he was talking to his dad. Two hours ago, they were making plans for Saturday. Two hours ago, everything was fine.

The room tilted. Spun. Nico's stomach lurched; he might throw up. His chest was too tight, lungs wouldn't expand. He tried to breathe, but nothing worked.

"Breathe," Gianna kept saying. "Please breathe." He dragged in air.

He was sobbing, snot and tears and sounds coming from his throat that he didn't recognize. His whole body shook with it. Gianna held him tight, and she was crying too, her tears hot against his shoulder, soaking through his t-shirt.

The Funeral.

The world continues as if nothing happened. The funeral was on Tuesday. Nico didn't remember most of it. Just fragments. The church was too crowded, too hot. The smell of lilies made him nauseous. The casket sat at the front, closed. Someone had said something about the impact, and Nico wished he could unhear it.

People kept touching him. Hugging him. Saying words:

"He was a good man."

"He's in a better place."

"Time heals all wounds."

Nico nodded. Said thank you. Felt nothing. Emma was there with her parents. She found him after, grabbed his hand.

"I'm so sorry," she said. Her eyes were red. She'd been crying too.

"Thanks for coming," Nico said almost dismissively.

One week later.

One week later, Nico returned to school. Everyone told him to take more time. But time didn't help. Sitting at home didn't help. Nothing helped.

The hallway parted around him like water. People he'd never spoken to looked at him with pity, then looked away quickly. No one knew what to say. At his locker, there was a card taped to the door. Sympathy card. Store-bought, generic flowers on the front.

Inside, careful handwriting: I'm so sorry about your dad. If you need anything, I'm here. - Jessica

Jessica from English class. Jessica, who sat at the lunch table he never joined. Jessica, who didn't know him but felt compelled to do something anyway.

He stared at it for a long moment. Closed his locker, card still inside, and went to class.

Between periods, he passed Trevor and Maya in the hallway. They were back together, making out by the same lockers where they'd fought last week. Trevor's hand was in Maya's hair. Her arms were around his neck. Completely absorbed in each other.

Trevor noticed Nico. Broke the kiss, awkward and uncomfortable.

"Oh. Hey, man. I, uh. Sorry about your dad."

"Thanks."

Nico nodded and kept walking. He was a shell walking through the building, going through motions, pretending to be human.

Five weeks later.

Five weeks after his father died, Nico found himself in his dad's study late at night. He couldn't sleep. Hadn't been sleeping. But his brain wouldn't stop. The study looked exactly as Luca had left it. The desk covered in papers. Laptop sitting closed, dormant. Nico sat down in his father's chair. It still smelled like him, coffee and aftershave and something else Nico couldn't name.

He opened the laptop. Password screen.

He tried Gianna's birthday. Wrong.

He tried her middle name. Wrong.

On impulse, barely thinking: His own birthday. 041719.

The screen unlocked. Nico's stomach turned.

Inside: files and files and files. Emails to his editor. Article drafts. Photos of construction sites. The wetlands story, laid out in meticulous detail. He opened the most recent email draft. Dated the night Luca died.

Subject: Wetlands Development - Ready to File

Hey Karen, I think I've got it. Attached is the full story... This is big.

The email never sent. Nico clicked on the attached article. Read through it. It was good. Thorough. Important. It wasn't a story worth dying for. It was just work. Just words on a page that never got published. A housing development that got built anyway. A pond that stayed polluted.

Nothing changed. His father died for nothing. The thought was suffocating.

Six weeks later.

Six weeks after his father died, Emma asked to meet at a coffee shop. Nico knew what was coming before she said it. He arrived late. She was already there, a cup of coffee and a green tea in front of her, untouched. Her eyes were red. He sat across from her. They both held cups; neither was drinking.

"I can't keep doing this," Emma said, voice breaking.

"I know."

"I love you. But you won't let me in."

"I know."

Her voice cracked more. "Is that all you're going to say?"

"I don't know what you want from me."

"Anything." She was crying now, openly. "Just—anything. Talk to me. Yell at me. Be angry. Be sad. Be something. But don't just... disappear while you're still sitting in front of me."

"I'm trying—"

"Are you?" Emma wiped her face with her sleeve. "When you're ready to talk, really talk, call me. I'll be there."

She stood. Nico didn't. She waited. One last chance for him to say something, anything. He stared at the floor. So she left.

Laura moved through the house like a ghost. Some days she'd come down for meals, sit at the table, and try to ask about school. Most days, she stayed in her room.

In early December, Laura called them down to the kitchen table.

"We're moving," she said.

"What?" Gianna asked.

"We can't stay here. I can't stay here. Everything in this house is—" She didn't finish. Didn't need to.

"Where?" Nico asked.

"Olympia. Washington. I have family there. We can start fresh."

"When?"

"After New Year's."

Late December.

Late December, Nico packed his room. On his desk, he found one of Emma's thumbtacks, one she'd left behind months ago. He held it in his palm. Remembered the weight of her head on his shoulder. Her fingers in his hair. He should throw it away. He put it in his pocket instead.

Gianna appeared in his doorway. "Ready?"

He looked around. "Yeah," he said.

On the Road.

The drive to Olympia was silent. Laura drove. Gianna in the passenger seat. Nico in the back with boxes stacked around him. Hours passed. Sacramento shrinking in the rearview mirror. Away from Emma. Away from school. Gianna reached back between the seats and found his hand. Nico closed his eyes. Tried not to think about his last conversation with his dad. About Emma's face when he pushed her away. Tried not to think about how fast good can disappear.

Olympia.

The new house was small. Suburbs. Unfamiliar. They unloaded boxes in silence. Nico's new room was smaller than his old one. Windows faced a different direction. Nothing was right. He stood in the middle of the empty room, boxes stacked around him, and thought: This is where I live now. This strange room in a strange city.

That night, he lay on an unmade bed. His phone was on the floor next to the bed. Messages he hadn't answered.

The flash drive was in his pocket. His dad's files. The story that killed him. The story that didn't matter. But if Nico figured it out. If he found what his dad was looking for. It may have meant something. Maybe his dad's death wasn't random. It wasn't meaningless.

The boy who had everything fall apart.

The boy who can't let go.

The boy who is drowning.

And refuses every hand extended to save him.
